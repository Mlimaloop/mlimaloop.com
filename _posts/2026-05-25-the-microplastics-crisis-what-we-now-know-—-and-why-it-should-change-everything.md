In 2004, a marine biologist named Richard Thompson published a paper in the journal Science that introduced a new term to the scientific vocabulary.

Microplastics.

Thompson and his colleagues had been studying beaches and seafloor sediments around the UK coast and had found something that had not been formally described before. Tiny fragments of plastic, less than five millimetres in size, present in the environment in quantities that suggested not recent contamination but accumulated degradation over decades. Larger plastic items, broken down by UV radiation, wave action, and abrasion, had fragmented into particles too small to see clearly with the naked eye but present everywhere they looked.

Twenty years later, the scientific understanding of microplastics has expanded beyond anything Thompson's original paper anticipated. The particles are not just on beaches. They are in the deepest ocean trenches. In Arctic sea ice. In the Pyrenees and the Alps, falling in rain at altitudes where human presence is minimal. In the soil of remote farmland. In the bodies of fish, birds, marine mammals, and insects across every ecosystem on earth.

And in us.

### What Microplastics Are

Microplastics are plastic particles smaller than five millimetres in their longest dimension. They are produced through two distinct pathways.

Primary microplastics are manufactured at small size for specific applications. Microbeads in cosmetics and personal care products. Plastic pellets, called nurdles, used as the raw material for plastic manufacturing that spill during shipping and handling. Synthetic fibres shed from textiles during washing. These enter the environment directly at their original small size.

Secondary microplastics are the fragments produced when larger plastic items break down in the environment. UV radiation weakens the polymer chains in plastic. Wave action, wind abrasion, and physical stress fracture the weakened material into progressively smaller pieces. A plastic bottle abandoned on a beach in 2000 has, by 2026, fragmented into millions of microplastic particles that are distributed across the surrounding environment.

Below five millimetres, the particles are microplastics. Below one micrometre, they are nanoplastics, invisible to the naked eye, capable of crossing biological membranes that larger particles cannot penetrate, and increasingly the subject of research into cellular and subcellular effects.

### Where Microplastics Have Been Found

The distribution of microplastics in the global environment is now understood to be essentially total. There is no ecosystem on earth that has been tested and found to be free of them.

In the ocean, microplastics are present throughout the water column from surface to seabed. The Mariana Trench, the deepest point on earth at nearly eleven kilometres below the ocean surface, contains microplastic particles. The concentration in surface waters of the world's oceans is estimated at over 170 trillion particles.

In freshwater systems, microplastics have been found in rivers, lakes, and groundwater on every continent. Lake Victoria, as we have written, contains microplastics in its fish tissue and water column. Rivers across East Africa carry microplastic loads from urban areas to the sea.

In the atmosphere, microplastics are transported by wind across enormous distances. Studies have found microplastic deposition in the French Pyrenees, in the Alps, in the Arctic, and in Antarctic snow. The particles travel in the same atmospheric systems that carry dust and pollen, settling wherever the air stills.

In soil, microplastics have been found in agricultural fields, forests, and remote wilderness areas. They alter soil structure, affect water retention, and disrupt the microbial communities that make soil productive. Estimates suggest there are more microplastics in global soils than in the oceans.

In food, microplastics have been found in fish, shellfish, honey, beer, tap water, bottled water, sea salt, and almost every other food product that has been systematically tested. The dietary exposure of humans to microplastics is now measured not as a risk to be assessed but as a present reality to be quantified.

### What Has Been Found in Human Bodies

This is where the science has moved fastest and most alarmingly in recent years.

In 2022, researchers published the first confirmed detection of microplastics in human blood. The study, conducted in the Netherlands, found microplastic particles in 77% of blood samples from healthy adult donors. The particles identified included PET, the material used in plastic bottles, polystyrene, and polyethylene. The concentrations found suggested ongoing exposure rather than single contamination events.

The same year, microplastics were detected in human lung tissue. Samples taken from patients undergoing thoracic surgery found microplastic particles in all regions of the lung, including the deepest sections where particle penetration had not been expected. The particles found included polypropylene, PET, and resin used in manufacturing processes.

In 2023, a study published in the New England Journal of Medicine found microplastic and nanoplastic particles in the carotid artery plaques of patients with cardiovascular disease. The patients whose plaques contained plastic particles had a significantly higher risk of heart attack, stroke, or death over a thirty-four month follow-up period than those whose plaques did not contain plastic. The relative risk increase was approximately 4.5 times. This was the first study to directly associate microplastic body burden with hard clinical outcomes in human patients.

Microplastics have been found in human placental tissue, detected on both the maternal and foetal sides of the placenta. They have been found in human breast milk. They have been found in the meconium of newborn infants, indicating that foetal exposure begins before birth.

The long-term health consequences of this accumulation are not yet fully understood. The honest scientific position is that the research is at an early stage relative to the scale of the exposure. But the early evidence, endocrine disruption, inflammatory response, cellular damage, cardiovascular risk, reproductive effects, is alarming enough to demand urgent attention.

### The East African Dimension

For communities in East Africa, the microplastics exposure burden is likely to be among the highest in the world, for reasons that compound across multiple exposure pathways.

Drinking water stored in plastic containers, which is standard practice across much of the region in the absence of reliable municipal supply, is a primary exposure route. Studies have found that water stored in PET bottles, particularly in hot conditions, contains higher concentrations of microplastic particles than water stored in glass or stainless steel. In Tanzania, where ambient temperatures are high and cold chain infrastructure is limited, the conditions for plastic leaching into stored water are consistently present.

Fish from Lake Victoria and the Indian Ocean, both of which are contaminated with microplastics, are primary protein sources for tens of millions of people across the region. Dagaa, the small sardine-like fish consumed whole and dried, offers no opportunity for consumers to avoid ingesting whatever particles are present in the fish's digestive system.

Open burning of plastic waste, which remains the dominant disposal method in communities without formal collection infrastructure, generates airborne microplastic particles and chemical compounds that are inhaled by the communities living nearest to the burning sites. The informal collectors who work with plastic waste daily have the highest direct exposure of all.
