There is a principle at the heart of the plastic crisis that most people instinctively understand but that has rarely been written into law.

The company that makes a product is responsible for what happens to it when the consumer is done with it.

Not the consumer. Not the municipality. Not the informal collector earning a dollar a day pulling bottles from a drainage channel. The company that designed the packaging, manufactured it, filled it, and sold it.

This principle has a name. Extended Producer Responsibility. And the countries and trading blocs that have written it into law with genuine teeth are the same ones with the highest recycling rates in the world. That is not a coincidence.

EPR is not a new idea. It is not a radical one. It is the logical application of the polluter pays principle to the packaging industry. And it is the single most powerful policy lever available to governments that want to build a recycling economy without funding it entirely from public money.

Here is what it actually means, how it works, where it already exists, and why its arrival in East Africa is a matter of when not if.

### What Extended Producer Responsibility Actually Is

In most economies today, the cost of managing packaging at end of life is borne by whoever is left holding it. Municipalities collect waste from households and pay for disposal. Informal collectors recover recyclable material and sell it for subsistence wages. Governments fund landfill sites and waste management infrastructure from general taxation.

The companies that produced the packaging in the first place bear none of these costs. The price of a bottle of water does not include the cost of collecting, sorting, and processing the bottle when it is empty. That cost is externalised onto society, onto the environment, and onto the people who happen to live near where the waste ends up.

Extended Producer Responsibility changes this by making producers financially responsible for the end of life management of the packaging they put into the market.

In its most basic form, EPR requires producers to either directly manage the collection and recycling of their packaging or to pay into a system that does it on their behalf. The fee they pay is typically calculated on the weight and type of packaging they sell into the market. Packaging that is harder to recycle pays a higher fee. Packaging that is easier to recycle pays less. This creates a direct financial incentive to design packaging for recyclability from the start.

The revenue generated by EPR fees funds the collection infrastructure, the sorting facilities, the processing capacity, and the logistics that make recycling work at scale. The system is self-funding. It does not require ongoing government subsidy. And it is paid for by the companies whose packaging created the problem in the first place.

### Where EPR Already Works

**The European Union**

The EU's Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation, updated and strengthened in recent years, is the most comprehensive EPR framework in the world. Every company selling packaged goods into the EU market is required to register with a Producer Responsibility Organisation and pay fees based on the weight and material type of their packaging.

The fees fund national collection and recycling systems across all 27 member states. Minimum recycled content requirements for PET beverage bottles are being phased in, starting at 25% recycled content and rising to 30% by 2030. These requirements create direct demand for certified rPET that producers must source from the recycling industry.

The EU EPR framework is not perfect. Implementation is uneven across member states. Some countries have well-functioning systems that meet targets. Others are behind. But the direction is unambiguous and the financial consequences of non-compliance are real.

**The United Kingdom**

The UK introduced its Extended Producer Responsibility for packaging legislation in 2024, requiring producers to pay fees that fund local authority collection and recycling costs. The UK Plastics Packaging Tax, introduced in 2022, charges producers and importers of plastic packaging that contains less than 30% recycled content. At the current rate of 217 pounds per tonne, this creates a direct financial incentive to source certified recycled material.

For a brand producing significant volumes of plastic packaging in the UK, the cost of not using recycled content is substantial. The cost of sourcing certified rPET, even at a price premium over virgin material, is often less than the tax liability for not doing so.

**Germany, Belgium, and the Nordics**

Several European countries had EPR frameworks in place long before the EU-wide legislation arrived. Germany's Green Dot system, introduced in 1991, was one of the earliest and most influential models. Belgium's Fost Plus and the Nordic country systems all operate on similar principles. These long-established frameworks have produced the recycling infrastructure and the certified supply chains that now serve as models for the rest of the world.

### Where EPR Is Coming

**Tanzania and East Africa**

Tanzania does not yet have a comprehensive EPR framework for packaging. But the direction of travel is clear.

The African continent is moving toward EPR legislation at an accelerating pace. South Africa introduced its Industry Waste Management Plans, a form of producer responsibility, in 2021. Kenya has been developing its EPR framework, with regulations covering packaging waste at an advanced stage of consultation. Rwanda, characteristically, is ahead of the regional curve with producer responsibility principles already embedded in its environmental legislation.

Tanzania's National Environmental Policy and the Environmental Management Act provide the legislative foundation for EPR. The specific regulations that would create a funded, fee-based producer responsibility system for packaging are in development. The timeline for full implementation is not certain but the political direction is established.

When Tanzania introduces EPR legislation, the companies selling packaged goods into the Tanzanian market will be required to pay for the collection and recycling of their packaging. That money will need to go somewhere. It will need to fund infrastructure. And the infrastructure it will need to fund most urgently is exactly what MlimaLoop is building.

### What EPR Means for Brands Right Now

For a global brand selling beverages, food, or consumer goods in markets with active EPR legislation, the situation is increasingly binary.

Either they source certified recycled content that meets minimum requirements and demonstrates genuine chain of custody, or they pay escalating levies and taxes for failing to do so. Either they partner with certified processors who can supply GRS-verified rPET with full documentation, or they face the regulatory and reputational consequences of not being able to demonstrate compliance.

The brands that are scrambling right now are not scrambling because they lack goodwill. Most of them made genuine commitments to recycled content years ago and believed supply would be available when their commitments came due. The problem is that supply has not kept pace with demand. The recycling infrastructure that needs to exist to deliver the certified material they need has not been built at the scale required.

In East Africa specifically, where several of the world's largest beverage brands sell enormous volumes into a market with no certified rPET supply chain whatsoever, the arrival of EPR legislation creates an urgent need that MlimaLoop is positioned to meet.

A brand like Coca-Cola Kwanza, selling millions of PET bottles into the Tanzanian market every year, will need to demonstrate either recycled content in its packaging or payment into a system that funds recycling of its packaging. The only way to demonstrate genuine in-market recycling of Tanzanian bottles is through a certified processing facility operating in Tanzania. There is currently no such facility.

There is a window here that will not stay open indefinitely.

The brands that establish supply chain partnerships with certified rPET processors before EPR legislation arrives in their markets are the ones that will meet compliance requirements smoothly. The brands that wait until legislation is live and then scramble for supply will pay premium prices into a seller's market, or face the regulatory consequences of non-compliance.
