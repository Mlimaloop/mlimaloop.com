Every serious infrastructure investment starts with a location decision. And every location decision is either made deliberately or by default.

MlimaLoop's location decision was deliberate.

Arusha is not the largest city in Tanzania. It is not the capital. It is not, by most conventional measures, the obvious choice for an industrial investment. Dar es Salaam, with its port access and larger urban population, would appear on the surface to be the more logical starting point for a recycling operation that ultimately needs to export certified material to global markets.

But location decisions are not made on surface logic. They are made on the specific requirements of the specific operation being built. And when those requirements are mapped against the geography, the economy, the infrastructure, and the strategic landscape of Tanzania, Arusha emerges not as a compromise but as the strongest possible starting point.

Here is the full case.

### Geography: The Intersection of Everything That Matters

Arusha sits at the foot of Mount Meru, in the shadow of Kilimanjaro, at the junction of the northern tourism circuit and the road networks connecting Tanzania to Kenya and the wider East African Community.

This geography is not incidental. It is the reason Arusha exists as a significant city and the reason it is the right place to start a recycling operation.

The northern tourism circuit, which connects Arusha to the Serengeti, Ngorongoro Crater, Lake Manyara, and Tarangire, generates enormous volumes of plastic waste. Hotels, lodges, camps, and the supporting supply chains that serve millions of tourists per year are consistent, high-volume generators of post-consumer PET. This is a feedstock stream that is geographically concentrated, relatively accessible, and in many cases generated by operators who have sustainability commitments and would actively prefer their plastic waste to enter a certified recycling stream rather than a landfill.

The Kilimanjaro climbing economy, which MlimaLoop's co-founder Zidane Juma knows intimately through Vertical Sky, generates a specific and significant plastic waste stream. Every climber who ascends Kilimanjaro carries water in plastic bottles. The majority of that plastic enters the informal waste stream at the base of the mountain. Formalising that collection, documenting it, and routing it through a certified processing facility is a natural alignment between the two ventures.

The road network connecting Arusha to Nairobi, two hundred and seventy kilometres to the north, is one of the most important trade arteries in East Africa. Finished rPET exported from Arusha can reach Mombasa port, East Africa's largest container port, via Nairobi in approximately six to eight hours of road freight. The logistics of getting certified output from Arusha's facility to a container ship are well understood and well-served by existing freight infrastructure.

### The Feedstock Case: Bottles That Are Already There

Any processing facility is only as viable as its feedstock supply. A recycling plant with insufficient or unreliable input material is an expensive building with idle equipment.

In Arusha, the feedstock case is strong for several interconnected reasons.

Tanzania produces approximately 30,200 tonnes of post-consumer PET bottles per year nationally, based on PETpro Tanzania's collection data. Arusha's population of approximately 1.8 million people in the greater urban area generates an estimated 52 tonnes of post-consumer PET bottles per month from water consumption alone, before soft drinks, juice, cooking oil, and other PET packaging are included.

The Muriet landfill on Arusha's outskirts already has approximately 400 informal collectors working it daily. These collectors exist because the material has value. The informal collection economy is already operating. MlimaLoop is not creating demand for collection from scratch. It is formalising and fairly compensating an activity that is already happening.

The tourism economy adds a feedstock stream that is not available in most East African cities at this density. Hotels and lodges on the northern circuit have sustainability reporting requirements driven by their international guests and operators. The ability to tell a hotel that its plastic waste is being collected, weighed, documented, and processed into GRS-certified rPET by a local facility is a proposition those hotels actively want. It is a supply chain partnership, not just a waste collection service.

### Infrastructure: The Non-Negotiables

The two infrastructure requirements that determine whether a recycling processing facility can operate are power and water. Both must be available at industrial scale, reliably, and at a cost that allows the operation to be commercially viable.

Arusha has 3-phase industrial power available through TANESCO, the Tanzania Electric Supply Company, with industrial connection points accessible in the city's established industrial areas including the Njiro and Usa River industrial zones. Power reliability in Arusha is imperfect, as it is across most of East Africa, but it is among the more reliable in Tanzania outside of Dar es Salaam.

Water supply through DAWASCO, the Dar es Salaam Water and Sewerage Corporation's northern equivalent, is available at industrial volumes in Arusha's established industrial zones. The processing facility's water requirement, primarily for the hot wash stage, is significant but manageable within the framework of a closed-loop water treatment system that recycles and retreats process water.

Industrial real estate in Arusha's established zones is available at costs significantly below comparable space in Dar es Salaam or Nairobi. The specific requirement for rPET processing, a large, high-ceilinged, single-storey industrial building with good vehicle access and proximity to utilities, is met by several available properties in the Njiro and Usa River areas.

### The Regulatory Environment

Tanzania's regulatory environment for industrial manufacturing and recycling, while not the simplest in the region, has well-established pathways for foreign-invested operations.

The Tanzania Investment Centre provides investor certification that grants import duty exemptions on processing equipment and formal government recognition of the investment. NEMC, the National Environment Management Council, has a regional office in Arusha that handles environmental impact assessments and operating permits. The existing presence of a small plastics processing operation in Arusha means the regulatory pathway is already understood by the relevant agencies.

Arusha's status as the headquarters of the East African Community, the regional economic bloc, gives it a particular significance in the regional regulatory landscape. EAC harmonisation of environmental and industrial standards is ongoing. Being located in the EAC headquarters city is not without strategic value as those harmonisation processes develop.

### The Human Capital Case

A recycling processing facility requires people. Specifically, it requires people who can operate industrial equipment, manage quality systems, supervise buying station operations, maintain chain of custody documentation, and interface with international buyers and certification auditors.

Arusha has a relatively well-educated urban workforce by regional standards, supported by several higher education institutions and a long history of engagement with international tourism, conservation, and development organisations that has built a base of English-speaking, internationally oriented professionals.

The specific skills required for rPET processing operations can be trained in-house. The supplier of the processing equipment provides commissioning support and operator training as part of the installation package. But the foundation of a capable, trainable workforce exists in Arusha in a way that it does not in smaller secondary cities.

Critically, Zidane Juma, MlimaLoop's co-founder, is Arusha-based. His deep roots in the local community, his relationships with the informal collector networks, his understanding of the local regulatory and business landscape, and his standing as a respected local figure are assets that cannot be replicated by choosing a different city. The human capital case for Arusha is, in part, a specific person case. And that is the strongest possible foundation for a community-embedded operation.

### The Strategic Case: First Mover in the Right Market

The strategic case for Arusha is ultimately about timing and positioning.

East Africa's certified rPET supply is currently zero. The first operational, GRS-certified rPET processing facility in the region will have a first-mover advantage that, in a capital-intensive, certification-dependent industry, is genuinely durable.

Arusha is not the only city in Tanzania where that facility could eventually be built. Dar es Salaam will almost certainly host a facility as the market develops. Mwanza, on Lake Victoria, has its own feedstock case. Mombasa in Kenya has port proximity that has its own logic.

But the first facility, the one that establishes the certification track record, the supply chain relationships, the collector network model, and the operational knowledge, has advantages that later facilities in later cities will not have. It sets the standard. It captures the early brand partnership relationships. It builds the team and the institutional knowledge that scales.

Building the first facility in Arusha, with the feedstock, the infrastructure, the regulatory pathway, the human capital, and the strategic alignment with Kilimanjaro tourism all pointing in the same direction, is the right call.

Not by default. By design.
