In 2022, a study published in the journal Environment International made headlines around the world. For the first time, researchers had detected microplastic particles in human blood. Not in people living near dump sites or working in plastics manufacturing. In ordinary people, going about their ordinary lives.

80% of the participants tested positive. The particles found included PET, the same polymer used to make plastic bottles.

It was a landmark moment. Not because it revealed something new about the plastic crisis, scientists had long suspected this was happening, but because it removed the last comfortable distance between "environmental problem" and "my problem."

Plastic is no longer something that happens to ecosystems and animals. It is inside us.

And in Africa, where plastic waste is most concentrated, where waste management infrastructure is least developed, and where communities have the least protection from exposure, the human cost is most acute.

## Who Bears the Burden

Before examining the specific health impacts of plastic pollution, it is important to be clear about something.

Africa contributes approximately 4% of global plastic production. The continent did not create the plastic crisis. The waste accumulating in African communities, waterways, and bodies largely consists of products manufactured elsewhere, sold through global supply chains, and inadequately managed in the absence of the recycling infrastructure that wealthy nations take for granted.

The communities most affected by plastic pollution in East Africa, the informal settlements on the edges of Dar es Salaam, Nairobi, and Kampala, the rural communities without waste collection, the fishing villages on plastic-contaminated coastlines are among the least responsible for the global plastic system that is harming them.

This is not a peripheral detail. It is the central moral context for everything that follows.

## Living With Plastic: The Community Impact

Across East Africa, millions of people live in proximity to informal waste dumps, plastic-littered waterways, and open burning sites. The visible impact on living conditions is severe and well-documented.

Plastic waste blocks drainage systems, creating the standing water that is a breeding ground for malaria-carrying mosquitoes. In Dar es Salaam, Nairobi, and Kampala, cities where malaria remains a leading cause of child mortality, studies have consistently linked plastic-blocked drains to elevated disease burden in adjacent communities.

Open burning of plastic waste, the dominant form of disposal in communities without formal collection, releases a toxic cocktail of airborne pollutants. Dioxins, furans, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, and fine particulate matter are all generated when plastic burns. Exposure to these compounds is associated with respiratory disease, cardiovascular disease, developmental disorders in children, and multiple forms of cancer.

Communities living within 500 metres of open dump sites experience elevated rates of respiratory illness, skin conditions, and low birth weight in newborns, all indicators of chronic toxic exposure. The people most affected are those with the least capacity to relocate, seek medical care, or advocate for change.

## Microplastics in the Human Body

The discovery of microplastics in human blood was significant. But subsequent research has revealed a contamination far more pervasive than a single study could capture.

Microplastics have now been detected in human lung tissue, liver tissue, kidney tissue, placental tissue, and breast milk. A 2023 study published in the New England Journal of Medicine found microplastic particles in the arterial plaques of patients with cardiovascular disease and found that patients with microplastics in their plaques had a significantly higher risk of heart attack, stroke, and death than those without.

The pathways of exposure are multiple. Drinking water, particularly water stored in plastic containers, which is standard practice across much of East Africa, is a primary source. Food contamination, particularly through fish and shellfish from plastic-contaminated water bodies, is significant. Inhalation of microplastic particles from the air, generated by the degradation of plastic waste in the environment, is increasingly recognised as a major exposure route.

For communities in East Africa, where plastic-contaminated water sources, fish from Lake Victoria and the Indian Ocean, and proximity to open burning sites create overlapping exposure pathways, the cumulative body burden of microplastic contamination is likely to be among the highest in the world.

The long-term health consequences of this level of exposure are not yet fully understood. But the early evidence, endocrine disruption, inflammatory response, cellular damage, reproductive effects, is alarming enough to demand urgent action.

## Children: The Most Vulnerable

Children are disproportionately affected by plastic pollution, and the data on childhood exposure in East Africa is deeply concerning.

Infants absorb microplastics through breast milk, itself contaminated from maternal exposure. Children playing in plastic-contaminated environments, which describes the reality for millions of children across East Africa, have elevated hand-to-mouth exposure and inhalation risk.

The developmental implications are significant. Several of the chemical compounds associated with plastic, phthalates, bisphenol A, flame retardants, are known endocrine disruptors. Exposure during critical developmental windows in childhood and in utero has been linked to hormonal abnormalities, reduced cognitive function, and behavioural disorders.

For a region already facing significant challenges in child health and educational outcomes, the additional burden of chemical exposure from plastic pollution is a compounding injustice.

## Food Security and Nutrition

The human health impact of plastic pollution extends beyond direct chemical exposure. It affects food security at a systemic level.

Soil contamination by microplastics reduces agricultural productivity, directly threatening the smallholder farming systems that feed the majority of East Africa's population. Fish contamination from marine and freshwater plastic pollution affects the nutritional quality and safety of a food source that hundreds of millions of people across the region depend on.

Perhaps most significantly, the decline of pollinator populations, driven in part by microplastic contamination of soil and flower surfaces, threatens the agricultural systems that depend on pollination. Research has found that microplastic exposure significantly reduces the survival and foraging efficiency of bee colonies. In East Africa, where smallholder agriculture is the foundation of food security and where bee populations are already under pressure, this is a threat with cascading consequences.

## The Informal Waste Sector

Any honest account of the human cost of plastic pollution in Africa must also address the people who work within it.

Across East Africa, millions of people work as informal waste collectors, sorting and selling recyclable material from dump sites, streets, and waterways. They work without protective equipment, without formal employment, without health coverage, and without recognition in any official economic accounting.

They are exposed daily to the toxic compounds released by plastic degradation and burning. They suffer elevated rates of respiratory disease, skin conditions, and infections from working with contaminated material. They are paid a fraction of the value of the material they recover, by intermediaries who capture the majority of the economic benefit.

## The Case for Infrastructure

The human cost of plastic pollution in Africa is not an accident. It is the predictable consequence of a system in which plastic is produced and consumed at scale, without the infrastructure to manage its end of life.

The solution is not behavioural change campaigns. It is not beach clean-ups. It is not asking communities who did not create the plastic crisis to individually solve it.

The solution is infrastructure. Formal collection. Industrial processing. Certified recycling. Systems that create economic value from waste and in doing so, remove it from the environments and the bodies of the people most harmed by its presence.
