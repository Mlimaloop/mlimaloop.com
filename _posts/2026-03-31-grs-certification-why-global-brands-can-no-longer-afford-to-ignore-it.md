If your brand has made a recycled content commitment — to consumers, to investors, to regulators — the question is no longer whether you need GRS certification in your supply chain. The question is how quickly you can get there.

The Global Recycled Standard is no longer a nice-to-have. It is the benchmark. And the brands that understand this earliest will be the ones with secure, verified supply chains when the rest of the market is scrambling.

## What GRS Actually Is

The Global Recycled Standard (GRS) is an international, voluntary product standard that sets requirements for third-party certification of recycled content and responsible social, environmental, and chemical practices in production.

In plain language: it is the internationally recognised framework that verifies a recycled content claim is real.

When a brand says its product contains recycled polyester — whether on packaging, in marketing, or in a sustainability report — GRS certification is what makes that claim credible, auditable, and defensible to regulators, investors, and consumers.

Without it, a recycled content claim is self-reported. With it, every kilogram of material has been independently verified from the point of collection through to the finished product.

## Why It Has Become Non-Negotiable

Three forces have converged to make GRS certification a commercial requirement rather than a voluntary choice.

**Regulatory pressure is intensifying.** The EU's Single Use Plastics Directive mandates recycled content targets for packaging. The EU Green Claims Directive requires substantiation of environmental claims — meaning brands cannot make recycled content statements without verified evidence. The UK Plastics Packaging Tax creates financial penalties for non-recycled content. Each of these frameworks points in the same direction: unverified claims are becoming a legal liability.

**Investor scrutiny is tightening.** ESG-mandated funds now require Scope 3 supply chain disclosure. Procurement from certified, traceable sources is increasingly the only way to satisfy investment portfolio reporting requirements. The question from investors is no longer "do you use recycled materials" — it is "can you prove it and where is the audit trail."

**Consumer trust is the product.** A generation of consumers has grown up with greenwashing fatigue. The brands that will win long-term are the ones whose sustainability claims hold up to scrutiny. GRS certification is the credibility layer that separates genuine commitment from marketing language.

## What GRS Certification Actually Requires

Understanding what GRS demands is essential for any brand assessing its supply chain.

**Chain of Custody.** Every organisation in the supply chain — from collector to processor to manufacturer to brand — must be GRS certified. A chain is only as strong as its weakest link. If your yarn supplier is certified but your rPET source is not, the claim breaks down at the first link.

**Minimum Recycled Content.** A product must contain a minimum of 20% recycled material to use the GRS label. For most brands making recycled content commitments, the target is far higher — but the standard sets the floor.

**Social and Environmental Requirements.** GRS is not purely a material standard. It includes requirements for safe and humane working conditions, environmental management systems, and restricted substance compliance. This means certification validates the integrity of the entire operation — not just the material.

**Annual Audits.** Certification is not a one-time event. Annual third-party audits are required to maintain certification. This keeps the standard meaningful and the claim current.

## What This Means for Your Procurement Strategy

For sustainability and procurement leads assessing supply chain options, the questions to ask any rPET supplier are straightforward:

Are you GRS certified — or on a verified certification pathway with a defined timeline?

Can you provide transaction certificates with every delivery?

Does your chain of custody documentation cover the full supply chain from collection to output?

Can you provide Scope 3 carbon data per tonne sourced for our emissions reporting?

These are not complex questions. But the answers will quickly separate credible suppliers from those who cannot support your compliance requirements.
