There is a particular kind of environmental initiative that feels good, photographs well, and changes very little.

The beach clean-up that removes two tonnes of plastic while thirty thousand tonnes flow past. The corporate pledge to use 30% recycled content by 2030 with no supply chain to back it up. The government awareness campaign with a catchy slogan and no infrastructure. The social media post about a turtle wrapped in a plastic bag, liked by millions of people who then go about their day unchanged.

These things are not wrong. They are insufficient. And the gap between what they offer and what the problem actually requires is the gap that nobody in the mainstream environmental conversation wants to measure honestly.

At MlimaLoop, we think the problem has layers. And naming those layers clearly, without the comfortable vagueness that lets everyone off the hook, is the first step toward doing anything useful about them.

So here is our honest account of what the problem actually is.

## Layer One: The Cities People Live In

Stand in almost any mid-sized city in Sub-Saharan Africa and look at the waste infrastructure around you. Or rather, look at the absence of it.

In Arusha, Tanzania, a city of over half a million people, the majority of residential areas have no formal waste collection service. Households accumulate waste and either burn it on site, dump it in drainage channels, or carry it to informal collection points where it may or may not be picked up on any given week. The formal collection system serves the city centre and the wealthier residential areas. It does not reach the majority of the population.

This is not an Arusha problem. It is a continental one.

Across Sub-Saharan Africa, urban waste collection coverage averages below 50% of the population. In many secondary cities it is below 20%. In informal settlements, which house the majority of urban residents across most East African cities, it is functionally zero.

The result is visible everywhere. Drainage channels filled with plastic. Rivers carrying waste loads that make them biologically dead for kilometres downstream. Open dump sites on the edges of communities, burning continuously, releasing toxic compounds into the air that residents breathe around the clock. Children growing up in environments where plastic is simply part of the landscape, as natural and as permanent as the soil.

This is not a developing world inevitability. It is a policy and investment failure. The cities exist. The people exist. The waste exists. The systems to manage it have simply never been built.

And as urbanisation across Africa accelerates, the continent's urban population is projected to triple by 2050, the gap between waste generated and waste managed is widening, not narrowing.

## Layer Two: The Economy That Exploits the People Who Try to Fix It

Here is something the clean-up campaigns do not show you.

The beach clean-up volunteers go home. The plastic they collected is photographed, logged, and disposed of through whatever channel is available. The informal collectors who were working that same beach before the volunteers arrived, and who will be working it after they leave, earn between one and two dollars a day for doing that work every single day without recognition, without protection, and without any of the economic value their labour generates.

The informal waste sector in East Africa employs millions of people. It diverts millions of tonnes of recyclable material from the environment every year. It does this with no government support, no formal infrastructure, and no fair payment system.

The price a collector receives for a kilogram of PET plastic is typically $0.07 to $0.11, paid by an aggregator who sells the same kilogram on for $0.15 to $0.22 and pockets the difference. The collector, who does the physical work in the most hazardous conditions, receives the smallest share of the value. The aggregator, who provides capital and transport, captures the margin. The processor, who adds the most technical value, earns the most.

This is not a free market failure. It is a structural one. Collectors have no price information, no alternative buyers, and in many cases no choice at all because they have taken advances from aggregators that keep them economically tied regardless of what price is offered.

In Arusha, researchers who asked landfill collectors what they earned were met with silence. Every single one refused to answer. In a controlled informal economy, sharing that information carries risk.

This is the human economy of plastic waste in East Africa. Millions of people doing essential environmental work, in genuinely dangerous conditions, for wages that keep them in poverty, within a system structured to ensure the value they create flows upward rather than to them.

Any initiative that does not address this layer is not solving the problem. It is photographing it.

## Layer Three: What Plastic Is Already Doing

The damage is not coming. It is here.

More than 50% of waste pickers in Tanzania earn below the poverty line doing work that directly benefits the global recycling supply chain. Children in communities adjacent to open dump sites grow up breathing air contaminated with dioxins, furans, and fine particulate matter from burning plastic. Studies have found elevated rates of respiratory illness, skin conditions, and low birth weight in newborns within 500 metres of open dump sites.

Microplastics have been detected in the muscle tissue of fish from Lake Victoria — a primary protein source for tens of millions of people across Tanzania, Kenya, and Uganda. They have been found in the blood of ordinary people not living near industrial sites. In the placental tissue of unborn children. In breast milk.

The Western Indian Ocean reef system, which supports the fisheries and tourism economies of Tanzania, Kenya, Mozambique, and the Seychelles, is experiencing accelerated coral bleaching and die-off driven in part by plastic contamination. Studies have found that the likelihood of coral disease increases from 4% to 89% when coral comes into contact with plastic.

Wildlife across East Africa is ingesting plastic. Livestock is dying from plastic impaction. Agricultural soil is accumulating microplastics that alter its structure, reduce water retention, and disrupt the microbial communities that make it productive.

This damage is not reversible on any timescale that matters to the people experiencing it today. The microplastics in the soil will be there for generations. The reef systems that die do not recover quickly. The health effects accumulating in communities living beside dump sites are already manifesting.

The question is not whether the damage has happened. It has. The question is whether we act now to stop it getting dramatically worse, or whether we continue at the current trajectory.

## Layer Four: What Happens If We Do Nothing

This is the layer that the comfortable version of the environmental conversation almost never reaches. Because the honest answer is deeply uncomfortable.

Here are the numbers.

Global plastic production in 1950 was approximately 2 million tonnes per year. By 2000 it had reached 213 million tonnes. By 2023 it had reached approximately 430 million tonnes. Current projections have it exceeding 700 million tonnes per year by 2040.

Global recycling rates have remained stubbornly low throughout this period. In 2023, approximately 9% of all plastic ever produced had been recycled. Around 12% had been incinerated. The remaining 79% — the overwhelming majority of every piece of plastic ever made — had been sent to landfill or released into the natural environment.

In Sub-Saharan Africa, recycling rates for plastic are below 4%. Collection rates in many cities are below 50%. The rest enters the environment.

Now apply the trajectory.

If African urban populations triple by 2050 as projected, and per capita plastic consumption increases even modestly as disposable incomes rise, the volume of plastic waste generated across the continent will increase by a factor of four to six times current levels.

If collection and processing infrastructure does not grow proportionally, and there is no current evidence that it will, the volume of plastic entering African waterways, soils, and communities will increase by the same factor.

The Indian Ocean, which already receives an estimated 20 billion tonnes of microplastic particles, will receive more. The communities living beside unmanaged dump sites, already bearing disproportionate health burdens, will bear more. The informal collectors already working in poverty will face more competition for material that is increasingly contaminated and decreasingly valuable as virgin plastic volumes overwhelm informal collection capacity.

Plastic does not biodegrade. It fragments. A bottle produced today will still be present in the environment in 2526. As microplastics, as nanoplastics, as particles invisible to the naked eye but present in the tissues of every living organism in the food chain.

The trajectory, if nothing changes, is not a gradual worsening. It is an acceleration. The volumes, the contamination, the health burden, and the economic damage all compound. And the window in which infrastructure built today can interrupt that trajectory is not indefinitely open.

## Where Does It Stop

It stops when the economics of plastic waste change.

Not when consumers feel guilty enough. Not when governments pass enough resolutions. Not when enough beaches are cleaned up for the cameras.

It stops when collecting plastic is more financially rewarding than abandoning it. When the people who do the physical work of recovery are paid fairly for it. When the material they collect has a formal, documented, certified pathway to global supply chains that value it. When the infrastructure exists to convert post-consumer waste into certified recycled material that brand partners need, legislation mandates, and investors can see returns from.

That is not an idealistic vision. It is a description of how recycling works in the countries where it works. Norway. Germany. Japan. The mechanism is not cultural or attitudinal. It is economic. When the incentive structures are right, the behaviour follows.

In East Africa, the incentive structures do not exist yet. The formal infrastructure does not exist yet. The certified supply chain does not exist yet.

But because we have looked at the layers of this problem clearly, done the maths on the trajectory, and concluded that the only thing that actually interrupts it is formal infrastructure that makes managing plastic more economically rational than abandoning it.

The problem is bigger than most initiatives are willing to say. The solution is more structural than most campaigns are willing to build.
