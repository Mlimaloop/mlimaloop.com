Pick up a clear plastic water bottle and hold it to the light. The material you are holding is polyethylene terephthalate, PET, and it is arguably the most remarkable packaging material ever created.

It is lighter than glass, stronger than most alternatives, chemically inert enough to safely contain food and drink, clear enough to show the contents, and — uniquely among common plastics, infinitely recyclable without significant loss of quality if processed correctly.

It is also, in most of the world, thrown away.

Understanding PET, where it came from, why it dominates global packaging, and what happens to it at end of life, is essential to understanding both the scale of the plastic crisis and the scale of the opportunity that MlimaLoop is built to capture.

## Where PET Came From

PET was first synthesised in 1941 by British chemists John Rex Whinfield and James Tennant Dickson, working at the Calico Printers' Association in Manchester. They were looking for a synthetic fibre to compete with nylon, and what they created, initially sold under the brand name Dacron in the US and Terylene in the UK, became the foundation of the global polyester textile industry.

For the first three decades of its commercial existence, PET was primarily a textile fibre. The lightweight, wrinkle-resistant properties that made it ideal for clothing, the polyester shirts that defined the 1970s were largely PET, also made it attractive to engineers looking for alternatives to glass in packaging.

The first PET beverage bottle was patented by Nathaniel Wyeth, brother of the painter Andrew Wyeth, in 1973. Wyeth, an engineer at DuPont, solved the problem of a plastic bottle that could withstand the pressure of carbonated drinks without deforming. The first commercial PET soft drink bottles appeared in 1977, and within a decade they had displaced glass across most of the global beverage market.

Today, approximately 500 billion PET bottles are produced globally every year. Roughly one million plastic bottles are sold every minute.

## Why PET Became Dominant

PET's dominance in beverage packaging is not accidental. It has a combination of properties that no other material matches across all the criteria that matter to manufacturers, retailers, and consumers.

It is lightweight, a PET bottle weighs roughly one tenth of the equivalent glass bottle, dramatically reducing transport costs and carbon emissions in distribution. It is shatterproof, unlike glass, a dropped PET bottle does not become a safety hazard. It is transparent, consumers can see the contents, which drives purchase decisions. It is chemically stable, it does not leach compounds into food or drink under normal conditions. It has an excellent barrier to gas and moisture, keeping carbonated drinks fizzy and still water uncontaminated for the shelf life required by retailers.

And it can be produced at scale with extraordinary precision. Modern PET bottle manufacturing, injection stretch blow moulding, can produce tens of thousands of bottles per hour from a single production line, to tolerances measured in fractions of a millimetre.

The result is the most successful packaging material in history. And the most ubiquitous piece of plastic waste on the planet.

## The Chemistry in Plain Language

PET is a polymer, a long chain molecule built by linking smaller molecular units together. The two building blocks of PET are purified terephthalic acid (PTA) and monoethylene glycol (MEG), both derived from petroleum feedstocks.

When these two molecules are combined under heat and pressure in the presence of a catalyst, they undergo a condensation reaction, linking together and releasing water, to form long chains of polyethylene terephthalate. The length and arrangement of these chains determines the physical properties of the final material.

The intrinsic viscosity (IV) of PET, a measure of how long the molecular chains are, is the key quality parameter in recycling. Virgin bottle-grade PET typically has an IV of around 0.78–0.84 dl/g. When PET is melted and reprocessed, the chains shorten slightly, reducing the IV. If the IV drops below a certain threshold, the material cannot be used for food-contact applications.

This is why Solid State Polymerisation (SSP) is the critical step in food-grade rPET production. The SSP reactor rebuilds chain length, raising the IV back to food-contact specification, allowing recycled PET to be used in applications that would otherwise require virgin material. It is also why MlimaLoop's facility specification includes an SSP reactor from day one.

## What Makes PET So Recyclable

Of all common plastics, PET has the most developed recycling infrastructure and the most established end markets. Three factors make it uniquely suited to recycling.

First, it is a single polymer. Unlike composite or multi-layer packaging, a PET bottle is made of one material, which means it can be melted and reprocessed without the contamination problems that plague mixed-material recycling.

Second, it retains its properties through multiple recycling cycles. Unlike some plastics that degrade significantly with each processing cycle, PET can be recycled repeatedly without losing the structural integrity that makes it valuable, provided the IV is maintained through SSP.

Third, there is a well-established global market for rPET. Brands need recycled content. Legislation is mandating it. The EU's packaging regulations require minimum recycled content in PET beverage bottles. The UK Plastics Packaging Tax creates financial incentives to use rPET. Global fashion brands have made 100% recycled polyester commitments that require rPET feedstock at massive scale.

Supply is the problem, not demand.

## The Waste Gap

Despite its recyclability, the majority of PET produced globally is not recycled. Global collection rates vary enormously, from over 90% in countries with deposit return schemes such as Norway and Germany, to under 5% across most of Sub-Saharan Africa.

The reason is not consumer behaviour. It is infrastructure.

In countries with deposit return schemes, the economic incentive to return bottles is built into the price of the product. Every bottle has a deposit attached to it. Return the bottle, get the deposit back. The result is near-total collection.

In countries without that infrastructure, which includes most of the world by population, the collection economics depend entirely on the informal waste sector. Collectors gather bottles because they can sell them to aggregators who sell them to processors. The price at each step in the chain determines how much material gets collected.

In East Africa, that price is $0.07–0.11 per kilogram paid to collectors by informal aggregators, below poverty line earnings for the collectors doing the physical work. The result is massive under-collection of a material that is both environmentally harmful in the environment and commercially valuable out of it.

## What rPET Becomes

The end markets for certified rPET are wide and growing.

Recycled polyester fibre, made by melting rPET and extruding it through fine spinnerets, is used in fleece jackets, sportswear, carpet, automotive interiors, and home textiles. One tonne of rPET produces approximately 700 kg of polyester fibre.

rPET flake and pellet for packaging, used to make new bottles, food trays, blister packs, and strapping. Food-grade rPET requires SSP processing to reach the IV and purity standards required for direct food contact.

rPET for industrial applications, used in geotextiles, construction materials, and industrial strapping where food-grade certification is not required.

The price premium for certified, traceable rPET over virgin PET in regulated markets is currently significant and growing, driven by legislation, brand commitments, and supply scarcity.
