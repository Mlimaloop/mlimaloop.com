### What GRS Certification Is

The Global Recycled Standard is an international, voluntary standard that sets requirements for third-party certification of recycled content in products and supply chains. It was developed by Control Union Certifications and is now administered by Textile Exchange, one of the world's leading textile sustainability organisations.

GRS certification applies to any product that contains recycled material — textiles, packaging, plastics, metals, paper. It covers the full supply chain from the point of recycling through to the final product, and it requires independent third-party auditing at every stage.

The standard has two core requirements. First, the recycled content claim must be accurate and verifiable. If a brand says its bottle contains 30% recycled PET, there must be an auditable chain of custody proving that claim from the collection point of the original post-consumer material through to the finished product. Second, the social and environmental practices of every facility in the supply chain must meet minimum standards covering chemical use, wastewater management, worker welfare, and environmental compliance.

GRS certification is not a self-declaration. A brand cannot simply state that it uses recycled content and claim GRS compliance. Every facility in the supply chain must be independently audited and certified by an accredited Certification Body. The chain of custody documentation must be maintained and auditable from collector intake through to finished product.

This is why GRS certification is genuinely meaningful and why brands that require it from their suppliers are making a substantive demand rather than a cosmetic one.

### Why Brands Need It

The reasons a brand needs GRS-certified supply rather than uncertified recycled content are multiple and increasingly non-negotiable.

**Regulatory compliance.** The EU's Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation requires minimum recycled content in PET beverage bottles, with percentages rising through the decade. Demonstrating compliance requires certified, auditable chain of custody documentation. Uncertified claims do not satisfy the regulatory requirement.

**Investor and reporting obligations.** Institutional investors and ESG rating agencies increasingly require brands to demonstrate their sustainability claims with third-party verified data. A recycled content claim without GRS certification or equivalent third-party verification is not credible for reporting purposes under frameworks like the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive.

**Consumer trust.** The greenwashing enforcement environment is tightening significantly. The EU's Green Claims Directive, which came into force in 2024, prohibits sustainability claims that cannot be substantiated with independent verification. A brand that claims recycled content without certification faces legal exposure in regulated markets.

**Supply chain transparency.** Major retailers are increasingly requiring their suppliers to demonstrate certified sustainable sourcing. A brand that cannot produce GRS certificates for its recycled content claims risks losing shelf space in premium retail channels that have adopted supplier sustainability requirements.

The cumulative effect of these pressures is that GRS certification, or an equivalent standard, has moved from a nice-to-have to a non-negotiable requirement for brands operating in regulated markets with serious sustainability commitments.

### What the Supply Chain Actually Has to Prove

This is where the commercial case for certified processors becomes concrete.

For a brand to make a GRS-compliant recycled content claim, every facility that handles the material from collection through to finished product must be GRS certified. That means the washing and processing facility. The extruder. The SSP reactor if food-grade material is being produced. The pellet packer and shipper.

It also means the chain of custody must be unbroken and auditable. The brand must be able to demonstrate, from the GRS transaction certificates it holds, that the recycled content in its product came from certified recycled material processed through certified facilities.

This is why a certified rPET processor is not interchangeable with an uncertified one. The brand's certification depends on the processor's certification. A brand that sources rPET from an uncertified processor in East Africa cannot make a GRS-compliant recycled content claim, regardless of how well-intentioned the processor's operation is.
