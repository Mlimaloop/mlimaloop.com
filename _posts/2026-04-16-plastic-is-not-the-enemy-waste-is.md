There is a version of the plastic conversation that is simple, emotionally satisfying, and almost entirely useless.

It goes like this: plastic is bad. We should use less of it. Ideally none. The corporations that make it are villains. The consumers who use it are complicit. Ban it, tax it, shame it out of existence.

This version of the conversation has dominated environmental messaging for thirty years. And in that time, global plastic production has more than doubled.

The problem with simple narratives is that they tend to be wrong. And when they are wrong at the scale of a global material crisis, the consequences of being wrong are severe.

Here is the more complicated, more honest, and ultimately more useful version of the plastic conversation.

## Plastic Has Saved More Lives Than It Has Harmed

Before we talk about what to do with plastic, we need to be honest about what plastic does.

The sterile packaging that keeps surgical instruments uncontaminated until the moment of use. The IV bags that deliver medication to patients in hospitals across the world. The blister packs that protect pharmaceutical tablets from moisture and contamination. The lightweight materials in vehicles and aircraft that reduce fuel consumption and carbon emissions. The insulation in buildings that reduces energy use. The pipes that deliver clean water to communities that would otherwise rely on contaminated surface sources.

These are not trivial applications. They are, in many cases, the difference between life and death at individual and population scale.

A 2020 study estimated that plastic packaging alone prevents approximately 1.7 kg of food waste for every 1 kg of plastic used in food preservation. In a world where food waste generates more greenhouse gas emissions than the entire aviation industry, that is not a small number.

Plastic is not inherently harmful. It is extraordinarily useful. The problem is not what it is. The problem is what we do with it when we are done with it.

## The Plastics That Should Not Exist

That said, not all plastic is equal. And some of it genuinely should not exist.

The category that deserves the harshest scrutiny is unnecessary single-use plastic. Packaging and products designed to be used once and discarded, for applications where durable or recyclable alternatives exist and are economically viable.

The plastic straw in a drink that could be served without one. The individually wrapped mint that did not need to be individually wrapped. The plastic bag given automatically at a checkout when the shopper already had one. The composite coffee cup that combines paper, plastic, and sometimes aluminium in a way that makes it unrecyclable, when a ceramic cup or a properly designed recyclable alternative would serve the same function.

These products exist primarily because they are cheap to produce and the cost of their disposal is externalised. Paid by municipalities, by communities, by the environment, by future generations rather than by the companies that made them or the consumers who used them.

The plastic bag ban in Tanzania is a good example of effective policy in this category. Where it has been enforced, it has demonstrably reduced the volume of plastic film entering the waste stream and the environment. It did not eliminate any essential function. It redirected behaviour toward alternatives that were always available.

This category is the legitimate target of elimination campaigns. And eliminating it is the right thing to do.

## The Plastics That Cannot Be Replaced

But elimination campaigns that extend beyond this category run into a wall of reality that they rarely acknowledge.

Try designing a sterile medical device without plastic. Try delivering clean water to a rural community without plastic pipe. Try manufacturing a modern electric vehicle, celebrated as the solution to fossil fuel dependence, without the plastic components that make up approximately 50% of its volume by parts count.

The lightweight plastics in aircraft reduce fuel burn and carbon emissions. The plastic composites in wind turbine blades make renewable energy infrastructure possible at scale. The plastic films in solar panels protect photovoltaic cells from moisture and extend their operational life.

Replacing these applications with alternative materials in most cases means heavier, more energy-intensive products with larger carbon footprints over their operational life. In some cases, no viable alternative exists at all.

This is the conversation that the simple anti-plastic narrative cannot have. Because the honest answer, that some plastic is irreplaceable and the world is materially better with it, does not fit the emotional logic of the campaign.

Acknowledging this is not a defence of the plastic industry. It is a precondition for designing solutions that actually work.

## The Plastics That Need Better Systems

Which brings us to the largest and most important category. The plastic that is neither unnecessary nor irreplaceable, but simply unmanaged.

PET beverage bottles. HDPE containers. PP packaging. The plastic that is genuinely recyclable, that has established end markets, that could in principle be collected, processed, and returned to productive use. But that in practice ends up in rivers, landfills, and open fires because the infrastructure to manage it does not exist.

This is the dominant category by volume. And it is the category where the plastic conversation most consistently fails to arrive at useful conclusions.

Because the problem here is not the plastic. The plastic is fine. A PET bottle is a well-designed, highly recyclable product that serves a genuine function. The problem is the complete absence of the systems needed to manage it at end of life.

In Norway, 92% of PET bottles are collected and recycled. The bottle is identical to the bottle sold in Tanzania. The difference is entirely infrastructural. A deposit return system that creates an economic incentive for return, combined with processing facilities capable of converting the collected material into certified rPET.

In Tanzania, less than 5% of PET bottles are collected and recycled. Not because the material is different. Not because consumers care less. Because the infrastructure does not exist.

Banning PET bottles in Tanzania would not solve the problem. It would eliminate a functional, recyclable product and replace it with alternatives that in the East African context have their own environmental footprints and supply chain complexities. The waste problem would not disappear. It would change shape.

Building the infrastructure to collect and process PET would solve the problem. It would turn waste into a valued material, create economic incentives for collection, generate livelihoods for the collectors who do the work, and supply global brands with the certified recycled content their legislation and commitments increasingly demand.

This is not a complicated insight. It is the obvious answer. And the one that receives by far the least investment and attention relative to the scale of the problem.

## What Should Actually Change in Plastic Production

If we accept this framework, eliminate the unnecessary, manage the unavoidable, build infrastructure for the recyclable, what does it actually demand from the companies that produce plastic?

First, design for recyclability from the start. A product designed with end of life in mind, single polymer where possible, avoiding carbon black pigments that defeat optical sorting, minimising adhesives that contaminate the recycling stream, is dramatically easier and cheaper to recycle than one designed purely for production efficiency and shelf appeal. This is not a radical ask. It is basic product design discipline applied to a previously ignored dimension.

Second, take responsibility for end of life costs. Extended Producer Responsibility is the policy mechanism that internalises the costs currently externalised onto communities and governments. Tanzania is moving toward EPR legislation. The EU already has it. The direction of travel is clear. The companies that build their operations around EPR compliance now will have a structural advantage over those that wait for it to be imposed.

Third, invest in or support recycling infrastructure in the markets where they operate. The brands that sell billions of PET bottles across East Africa and then disclaim responsibility for what happens to those bottles are operating on borrowed time, environmentally, reputationally, and regulatorily. The brands that partner with processors like MlimaLoop to create certified, traceable recycling supply chains in the same markets where they sell are building the infrastructure that protects their business long term.

## The Position MlimaLoop Takes

We are not anti-plastic.

We are anti-waste. Anti-mismanagement. Anti the fiction that banning things is the same as solving things.

PET plastic is a remarkable material. It is lightweight, strong, chemically stable, endlessly recyclable, and in acute global supply shortage in its certified recycled form. The brands that need it cannot get enough of it. The legislation that mandates it is accelerating. The investment community that finances it is growing.

The problem is not the material. The problem is that in East Africa, and across most of the developing world, the infrastructure to collect it, process it, and return it to the supply chain simply does not exist.

MlimaLoop exists to build that infrastructure. Not to campaign against plastic. Not to demand that the world use less of something it demonstrably needs. But to ensure that what is produced is managed, fairly, formally, and at scale, so that the material that enters our environment today does not stay there for five hundred years.

The enemy is not plastic. The enemy is the absence of systems to manage it.
