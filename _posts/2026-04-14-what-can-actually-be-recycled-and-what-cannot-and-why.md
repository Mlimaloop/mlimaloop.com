There is a word that has done enormous damage to the global recycling system. That word is wishcycling.

Wishcycling is what happens when someone puts something in a recycling bin hoping it will be recycled, without knowing whether it actually can be. The yoghurt pot with residue still inside. The greasy pizza box. The crisp packet. The coffee cup. The black plastic tray. The lid that might be a different plastic to the container.

Wishcycling feels virtuous. It is, in practice, often harmful. Contamination from non-recyclable materials entering the recycling stream drives up processing costs, reduces output quality, and in some cases renders entire batches of otherwise recyclable material worthless.

Understanding what can actually be recycled, and why, is one of the most practically useful pieces of environmental knowledge a person can have. Here is the honest guide.

## The Fundamental Rule

Before the specifics, the rule that governs everything: recyclability is not just about the material. It is about the material, the condition of the material, and whether recycling infrastructure exists in your location to process it.

A PET bottle is technically recyclable. A PET bottle filled with motor oil is not, the contamination cannot be removed economically. A PET bottle in Norway has a 92% chance of being recycled. A PET bottle in Tanzania has a less than 5% chance, not because it is different plastic, but because the infrastructure to collect and process it barely exists.

This distinction matters enormously for policy, for investment, and for individual behaviour. The solution to low recycling rates is almost never education campaigns telling people to recycle better. It is infrastructure that makes recycling economically viable and physically accessible.

## What Can Be Recycled — Reliably

**PET bottles (resin code 1).** Clear PET bottles, water, soft drink, juice, cooking oil, are the most reliably recycled plastic in the world. Remove the cap if it is a different plastic. Rinse briefly. Compress if possible to save space. This is the most valuable material in the informal recycling economy and the one with the most established global end markets.

**HDPE containers (resin code 2).** Milk bottles, detergent bottles, shampoo bottles. Rinse and leave the cap on, most HDPE caps are also HDPE and can be processed together. HDPE has good recycling economics and established end markets in pipes, garden furniture, and new containers.

**Aluminium cans.** Aluminium is infinitely recyclable with no loss of quality. It requires 95% less energy to recycle aluminium than to produce virgin aluminium from bauxite. An aluminium can recycled today can be back on a shelf as a new can within 60 days. In East Africa, aluminium has significant informal market value and is actively collected.

**Cardboard and paper.** Clean, dry cardboard and paper, boxes, newspapers, office paper, have well-established recycling markets. Contamination with food or liquids is the main problem. A wet cardboard box is generally not recyclable because the fibres are damaged and the contamination cannot be removed.

**Glass bottles and jars.** Glass is infinitely recyclable and retains full quality through recycling. The economics of glass recycling depend heavily on transport costs, glass is heavy, but in markets where collection infrastructure exists, glass has strong end markets.

## What Cannot Be Recycled, At Any Scale

**Multi-layer packaging.** Coffee cups, juice cartons, crisp packets, and most flexible food packaging combine multiple materials, plastic film, aluminium foil, paper, laminated together in ways that cannot be separated with current technology. These materials are not recyclable. They are destined for landfill or incineration regardless of which bin they go in.

**Black plastic trays.** Black plastic, typically PP or PET, cannot be identified by the optical sorting systems used in most recycling facilities because the carbon black pigment that makes it black absorbs the infrared light used for identification. Black plastic passes through sorting facilities as contamination and ends up in landfill.

**Flexible plastic film.** Plastic bags, cling wrap, bread bags, and other film plastics clog sorting machinery and are generally not accepted in kerbside recycling. Some supermarkets in developed markets have film take-back schemes, but at scale, flexible film has very limited recycling end markets.

**Polystyrene (resin code 6).** Expanded polystyrene, takeaway cups, packaging foam, is 98% air by volume. The economics of collecting and transporting it are almost never viable. Rigid polystyrene cutlery and containers have slightly better economics but almost no collection infrastructure in East Africa.

**Contaminated materials.** A recyclable material that is contaminated with food, liquid, chemical residue, or non-compatible materials may not be recyclable in practice. Contamination drives up processing costs and reduces output quality. A small amount of contamination in a batch can require the entire batch to be disposed of as waste.

**Composite items.** Anything that combines materials that cannot be separated, a plastic-handled metal tool, a padded envelope with bubble wrap, a toy with mixed components, is generally not recyclable through standard streams.

## The Grey Areas

**Pizza boxes.** The top of a pizza box is usually clean cardboard and can be recycled. The bottom, soaked with grease, generally cannot. Tear them apart and recycle the clean half.

**PET pots, tubs, and trays.** Many PET containers that are not bottles, fruit punnets, salad trays, some food containers, are technically the same material as a bottle but are not collected or recycled in most markets. The problem is sorting, facilities optimised for bottles often cannot process the different shapes. In East Africa, collection currently focuses on bottles because the informal economics are clearest for a recognisable, consistent format.

**Bioplastics.** Materials labelled compostable or biodegradable, often used in coffee cups, cutlery, and packaging, are not recyclable and are not compatible with standard composting streams. They require industrial composting at specific temperatures and humidity levels to break down. In most contexts, they end up in landfill where they behave identically to conventional plastic.

**Bottle caps.** This depends on the cap material and your location. PP caps on PET bottles, which is the most common combination, can be processed separately if the facility has the capability. MlimaLoop's facility separates caps in the sink-float tank. In markets without that infrastructure, caps are often removed and discarded because mixing them contaminates the PET stream.

## What This Means in East Africa

In the East African context, the practical answer to "what can be recycled" is currently narrow, but it is exactly the materials that MlimaLoop focuses on.

Clear PET bottles. HDPE containers. Aluminium cans. PP caps as a byproduct of PET processing. These are the materials with established collection economics, established end markets, and the technical characteristics that make processing viable.

Everything else, the multi-layer packaging, the black plastic, the polystyrene, the film, represents the next generation of recycling challenges. Solutions exist for some of them in advanced markets. In East Africa in 2026, the priority is building the infrastructure for the materials where the case is already proven.

Get PET right first. Then the rest follows.
