There is a question that every investor asks when they first encounter the rPET opportunity, and it is the right question to ask.

If recycled plastic costs more to produce than virgin plastic made from cheap petrochemical feedstock, why would any brand choose to buy it?

The answer has several layers. And understanding those layers is the key to understanding why the rPET price premium exists, why it is growing, and why the economics of certified rPET processing in East Africa are more compelling than they appear at first glance.

### What Virgin PET Actually Costs

Virgin PET is made from two petrochemical feedstocks, purified terephthalic acid and monoethylene glycol, both derived from petroleum and natural gas. The price of virgin PET is therefore closely linked to oil prices, with additional influences from feedstock processing capacity, shipping costs, and regional supply and demand dynamics.

In global commodity markets, virgin PET has historically traded at between $800 and $1,400 per tonne depending on oil prices and market conditions. When oil is cheap, virgin PET is cheap. When oil prices rise, virgin PET prices follow.

For a brand buying PET resin to manufacture bottles, virgin material has historically been the default choice not because it is environmentally preferable but because it is familiar, consistently available, and in periods of low oil prices, inexpensive.

### What Certified rPET Actually Costs

Certified rPET, produced from post-consumer bottles through a mechanical recycling process and independently verified through GRS certification, trades at a premium to virgin PET. The size of that premium varies by grade, certification level, and geography, but has generally sat at between $100 and $400 per tonne above virgin PET prices in recent years.

For food-grade rPET, produced through the additional SSP process that raises intrinsic viscosity to food-contact specification, the premium is at the higher end of that range. For non-food-grade rPET pellet, suitable for packaging, strapping, and industrial applications, the premium is lower but still meaningful.

The premium exists for several interconnected reasons.

Collection and processing costs are higher than petrochemical feedstock costs. Collecting post-consumer bottles from dispersed sources, sorting, washing, and processing them into specification-grade material requires more labour, more infrastructure, and more quality management than manufacturing virgin resin from a continuous petrochemical process.

Certification costs add to the base processing cost. GRS auditing, chain of custody management, quality testing, and the documentation systems required to maintain certification are real costs that virgin material producers do not bear.

Supply is genuinely constrained. Collection rates for post-consumer PET remain well below 100% in most markets. Even in high-performing markets like Germany and Norway, the available post-consumer feedstock is finite. Certified, GRS-verified supply is a subset of total recycled supply. The brands that need it are competing for a limited pool.

### Why the Premium Is Growing

The rPET price premium is not static. It has been growing and the forces driving it are structural, not cyclical.

**Legislative demand is creating a floor.**

The EU's minimum recycled content requirements for PET beverage bottles create guaranteed demand for certified rPET that does not exist at the discretion of individual brands. When 25% recycled content is legally mandated, brands cannot choose to buy virgin material instead regardless of price differential. The demand is locked in by law.

As those minimums rise to 30% and eventually higher, the volume of certified rPET legally required by the EU market alone increases every year. Supply has not kept pace. The structural deficit between mandated demand and available certified supply creates upward pressure on prices that is independent of oil price movements.

**Brand commitments are creating additional demand above the legislative floor.**

Many brands have made voluntary recycled content commitments that exceed legal minimums. Driven by investor pressure, consumer expectations, and competitive positioning in sustainability rankings, these commitments add demand above and beyond what legislation requires.

When a brand commits to 50% recycled content across its entire PET portfolio and the legal minimum is 25%, it is voluntarily creating demand for twice the legally required volume of certified material. Multiplied across hundreds of global brands making similar commitments, the voluntary demand layer is substantial.

**The Plastics Packaging Tax and equivalent mechanisms are making the premium economically rational.**

In the UK, a brand that uses less than 30% recycled content in its plastic packaging pays £217 per tonne in tax. If certified rPET costs £200 per tonne more than virgin PET, using certified recycled content is not just environmentally preferable. It is the cheaper option after tax. The premium effectively disappears when the tax liability for not paying it is factored in.

As similar mechanisms are introduced in other markets, the economic calculation that once made virgin PET the default choice shifts decisively. The premium for certified rPET becomes, in regulated markets, not a cost but a saving.

**Oil price volatility is making virgin PET less predictable.**

The petrochemical feedstocks that determine virgin PET prices are subject to geopolitical disruption, OPEC production decisions, refinery capacity constraints, and the long-term structural pressure of decarbonisation on the oil and gas industry. Virgin PET prices can move dramatically in short periods.

Certified rPET, priced primarily on collection and processing costs rather than oil prices, offers brands a degree of price stability that virgin material cannot. For a brand managing packaging costs across a multi-year budget cycle, the predictability of rPET pricing has value beyond the material cost itself.

### Why East Africa Changes the Economics

Here is where the MlimaLoop opportunity becomes particularly interesting from a commercial perspective.

The rPET price premium exists in part because collection and processing costs in the markets where most certified rPET is currently produced, Western Europe and parts of Asia, are high. Labour costs are high. Real estate costs are high. Regulatory compliance costs are high.

In East Africa, those costs are structurally lower.

Collector labour costs in Tanzania are a fraction of European equivalents. Industrial real estate in Arusha is dramatically cheaper than in Germany or the Netherlands. The feedstock, post-consumer PET bottles, is available at $0.14 per kilogram compared to the $0.20 to $0.35 per kilogram that European processors typically pay for post-consumer material.

This means that a certified rPET processor operating in East Africa, with lower input costs across the board, can produce certified material at a lower cost base than European competitors, while still selling into global markets at the premium price that certification commands.

The margin available to an East African certified rPET processor is therefore structurally more attractive than the margin available to a European one. Not because of any subsidy or artificial advantage, but because the underlying economics of collection and processing in a lower-cost environment are genuinely better.

This is the commercial case for MlimaLoop that is often missed in the first conversation. People see a recycling operation in Tanzania and think development economics. What they should see is a premium certified materials business with a structurally lower cost base than its European competitors, selling into a global market where supply is constrained and demand is legislatively mandated.

### What the Market Looks Like in 2030

By 2030, the EU's mandatory recycled content requirements will be fully in force across all member states. The UK's Plastics Packaging Tax will have been operating for eight years. EPR legislation will be active or imminent across East Africa. The voluntary commitments made by global brands in the early 2020s will be reaching their stated deadlines.

The global certified rPET market, currently valued at approximately 57 billion dollars per year and growing, will be significantly larger. The supply deficit that currently exists will either have been addressed by new processing infrastructure or it will have widened further as demand continues to grow faster than collection and processing capacity.

The processors that are operational, certified, and supplying into established brand relationships in 2030 will have a structural advantage that later entrants cannot easily replicate. First-mover advantage in a capital-intensive, certification-dependent industry is durable. The relationships, the certification track record, the supply chain integrations, and the operational knowledge accumulate over time and become genuine competitive moats.
