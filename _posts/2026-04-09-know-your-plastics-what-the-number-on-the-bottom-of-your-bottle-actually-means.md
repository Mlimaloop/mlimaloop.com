Turn over almost any plastic item in your home and look at the bottom. Somewhere on the base you will find a small triangle made of arrows, with a number inside it, usually between 1 and 7. It might be on your water bottle, your yoghurt pot, your shampoo bottle, your takeaway container.

Most people have never noticed it. Almost nobody knows what it means.

That number is a resin identification code, a classification system introduced in 1988 to help recyclers identify plastic types. It is not a recycling symbol, despite what the arrows suggest. It does not mean the item is recyclable. It simply tells you what the plastic is made of.

And what it is made of determines almost everything, whether it can be recycled, how many times, into what, and whether any recycling infrastructure in your region is capable of processing it.

Here is what each number actually means.

## 1 — PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate)

PET is the plastic used for water bottles, soft drink bottles, juice bottles, cooking oil containers, and some food packaging. It is clear, lightweight, strong, and, critically, highly recyclable.

PET was first developed in the 1940s by British chemists working for Imperial Chemical Industries. Commercial production for beverage bottles began in the 1970s, and it became the dominant packaging plastic for liquids globally within two decades.

It is the most widely recycled plastic in the world, and the core material of MlimaLoop's operation. When PET is recycled correctly, it can be converted into rPET flake, pellet, food-grade resin, or recycled polyester fibre. A bottle collected in Arusha today can become a fleece jacket, a carpet tile, a food container, or another bottle within months.

Recycling status: highly recyclable, the gold standard.

## 2 — HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)

HDPE is a stiffer, more opaque plastic used for milk jugs, detergent bottles, shampoo bottles, and plastic bags. It is durable, chemical-resistant, and relatively easy to recycle.

HDPE was first produced in the 1950s by scientists at Phillips Petroleum. Its strength-to-weight ratio made it immediately attractive for industrial and consumer packaging.

It is widely recycled in developed markets, processed into pipes, garden furniture, bins, and new bottles. In East Africa, HDPE recycling infrastructure exists but is patchy. The material has value in the informal market and is commonly collected alongside PET.

Recycling status: widely recyclable where infrastructure exists.

## 3 — PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)

PVC is used for pipes, window frames, flooring, cling film, and some food packaging. It is highly durable and weather-resistant, which makes it extremely useful in construction, and extremely problematic in waste management.

PVC was first synthesised in the 1870s but commercialised in the 1920s. It became one of the most widely produced plastics globally, particularly in construction applications.

The problem with PVC is what happens when it is processed or burned. It releases chlorine-based compounds including dioxins and furans, some of the most toxic substances known. Recycling PVC is technically possible but economically very difficult, and most recycling facilities will not accept it. When burned in open dumps, as happens routinely across East Africa, it releases those toxic compounds directly into the air.

Recycling status: rarely recyclable. Avoid where possible.

## 4 — LDPE (Low-Density Polyethylene)

LDPE is a softer, more flexible plastic used for plastic bags, cling wrap, squeezable bottles, and some food packaging films. It was the first polyethylene to be produced commercially, developed by ICI in the UK in 1933.

LDPE is technically recyclable but practically very difficult. It is lightweight, which makes collection economics poor. It clogs sorting machinery at recycling facilities. And it is often contaminated with food residue that makes cleaning difficult.

In most East African markets, LDPE has little or no formal recycling value. Plastic carrier bags, primarily LDPE, are the visible face of the plastic pollution crisis across the region, accumulating in waterways, farmland, and wildlife areas. Tanzania has a plastic bag ban in place, but enforcement remains patchy and LDPE film continues to enter the waste stream in large volumes.

Recycling status: technically recyclable but rarely processed. Reduce and avoid.

## 5 — PP (Polypropylene)

PP is a hard, semi-rigid plastic used for yoghurt pots, margarine tubs, bottle caps, straws, food containers, and medical equipment. It has a high melting point, which makes it suitable for hot-fill applications and microwave-safe containers.

PP was developed in 1951 by two chemists working independently, Karl Ziegler in Germany and Giulio Natta in Italy, who shared the Nobel Prize in Chemistry for its discovery in 1963.

PP is increasingly being recycled as infrastructure develops, melted down into granules and reprocessed into bins, automotive parts, and new containers. MlimaLoop's processing facility separates PP bottle caps from the PET bottle stream using a sink-float water tank, PET sinks, PP floats, and the PP caps become a separate revenue stream through granulation.

Recycling status: recyclable and increasingly valuable. Separated at MlimaLoop's facility.

## 6 — PS (Polystyrene)

PS is used in two main forms: rigid polystyrene for cutlery, CD cases, and some food containers, and expanded polystyrene (EPS or Styrofoam) for cups, takeaway containers, and packaging foam.

Polystyrene was first produced commercially in the 1930s by BASF in Germany. Expanded polystyrene, the white foam, was developed in the 1950s.

PS is one of the most difficult plastics to recycle economically. Expanded polystyrene is 98% air by volume, which makes transport costs prohibitive relative to material value. Rigid PS can be recycled but collection infrastructure is almost entirely absent in East Africa. When burned, PS releases styrene and other toxic compounds.

Recycling status: rarely recyclable in practice. One of the most problematic waste plastics.

## 7 — Other

Resin code 7 is a catch-all category covering everything that does not fit into codes 1 through 6, including polycarbonate (PC), bioplastics (PLA), acrylic, nylon, and multi-layer composite materials.

This category is the most complex and generally the least recyclable. Polycarbonate, used in some water bottles and baby bottles, contains bisphenol A (BPA), a known endocrine disruptor that has prompted regulatory restrictions in many countries. Bioplastics labelled as compostable often require industrial composting conditions to break down and will not degrade in a home compost bin or landfill.

Multi-layer composite packaging, juice cartons, coffee cups, crisp packets, combines different materials laminated together in ways that cannot be separated, making recycling essentially impossible with current technology.

Recycling status: almost never recyclable. Avoid where possible.

## Why This Matters

The resin identification system was designed for industrial recyclers, not consumers. But understanding it changes the way you engage with plastic, as a buyer, as a business, and as a policymaker.

The single most important thing to know: number 1, PET, is the one that works. It is the most collected, most processed, most valuable post-consumer plastic in the world. Every PET bottle correctly collected and recycled stays out of the environment, out of the ocean, and out of the food chain.

In East Africa, where formal recycling infrastructure barely exists for any plastic type, PET is where the economic case for investment is clearest and the environmental benefit is most immediate.
