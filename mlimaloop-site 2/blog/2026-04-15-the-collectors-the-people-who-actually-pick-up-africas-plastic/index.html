The Collectors: The People Who Actually Pick Up Africa's Plastic

Every piece of plastic that gets recycled in East Africa passes through the hands of an informal collector first. They are the first and most essential link in the recycling chain. Here is who they are, what their lives actually look like, and why MlimaLoop exists to change their economic reality.

By MlimaLoop Team
MLIMALOOP

Before a plastic bottle becomes rPET fibre, before it enters any supply chain or satisfies any brand's recycled content commitment, it passes through a pair of human hands.

Those hands belong to an informal collector. A waste picker. Someone who walks the streets, searches the dump sites, and wades through drainage channels collecting the material that everyone else has thrown away.

In East Africa, there are millions of them. They are the invisible infrastructure of the recycling economy, responsible for recovering the overwhelming majority of whatever plastic gets recycled at all. Without them, the recycling system does not exist. With them, it barely functions.

This is their story. And it is the reason MlimaLoop exists.

Who the Collectors Are

The informal waste collection sector in East Africa is not a monolithic workforce. It is a diverse, stratified, largely ungoverned economy made up of individuals with very different circumstances, operating at very different scales.

At the base are the street collectors, men, women, and in too many cases children, who work on foot through urban neighbourhoods, market areas, and roadsides gathering whatever recyclable material they can find and carry. They work with their hands, often without gloves. They carry material in sacks on their backs, sometimes covering ten or fifteen kilometres a day. They sell to whoever is buying, typically a local aggregator or middleman, at whatever price is offered that day.

Above them are the dump site workers, collectors who operate at landfills and open dump sites where material is more concentrated. The work is physically harder and the environmental exposure more severe, but the volume of collectable material is higher. At Arusha's Muriet landfill, approximately 400 people work the site on any given day, sorting through incoming waste as trucks arrive.

Above them are the aggregators, individuals who have accumulated enough capital to buy material from multiple collectors and sell it on in volume to processors or exporters. They sit between the collectors who do the physical work and the processors who create the value, and they capture a disproportionate share of the economic benefit from both.

What They Actually Earn

More than 50% of waste pickers in Tanzania earn between $1.11 and $1.85 per day, below the World Bank poverty line of $2.15 per person per day.

The earnings data from Tanzania's informal waste sector is deeply uncomfortable reading.

For people doing physically demanding, environmentally hazardous work that serves a genuine environmental function, this is not a living wage. It is subsistence.

The better-performing collectors, those with established routes, reliable aggregator relationships, or access to higher-value material streams, can earn $7 to $10 per day in markets like Dar es Salaam. But these are outliers. The majority operate well below this level.

The price a collector receives per kilogram of PET plastic from an informal aggregator is typically $0.07 to $0.11. For a collector working a full day and gathering 20 kilograms of bottles, which represents a meaningful physical effort, that is $1.40 to $2.20 for an entire day's work.

The aggregator who buys that material at $0.07 to $0.11 per kilogram then sells it on to a processor at $0.15 to $0.22 per kilogram. The margin between what the collector earns and what the aggregator receives is where the informal economy extracts its most egregious toll.

The Debt Trap

In Arusha, research into the informal waste trade found something more troubling than simple underpayment.

The system is structured around debt.

Large dealers, the aggregators who sit at the top of the informal collection hierarchy, provide collectors with advance capital to fund their collecting activities. The collector uses that capital to operate, then sells their material back to the dealer who provided the advance. After the dealer recovers the capital, the collector receives the difference.

This arrangement sounds like credit. In practice it functions like bondage. A collector who has taken an advance from a dealer cannot freely sell to another buyer without first clearing the debt. They are economically tied to whoever extended them credit, at whatever price that person chooses to pay.

It is a system designed to keep collectors dependent, to suppress the price paid for their labour, and to ensure that the people doing the most physically demanding work in the recycling chain receive the least of the economic value it generates.

What the Work Actually Looks Like

A street collector in Arusha wakes before dawn. They cover their route early, before the heat and before other collectors have worked the same areas. They wear whatever they have, sandals on some of the most contaminated ground in the city. They sort through waste with their bare hands, distinguishing PET from HDPE, identifying metal, separating material by value. They load sacks that can weigh thirty, forty, fifty kilograms and carry them on their backs or push them on improvised carts.

They work in environments contaminated with industrial chemicals, medical waste, rotting organic matter, and the toxic residue of burned plastic. They have no occupational health coverage. No sick pay. No insurance. No pension. No formal recognition in any government labour statistics.

At the end of the day they sell their load to an aggregator at a price they did not set and cannot negotiate. They go home, return tomorrow, and do it again.

The collector at the Muriet landfill in Arusha who was asked about their earnings by researchers declined to answer. So did every other collector at the site. Not because they had nothing to say, but because in a controlled informal economy, sharing information about what you earn is not safe.

That silence tells you everything.

The Systemic Problem

Informal waste collectors are not a symptom of a broken system. They are the system, functioning at enormous scale with almost no formal support, recognition, or protection.

Across East Africa, informal collectors divert millions of tonnes of recyclable material from landfills every year. They do it without government funding, without formal infrastructure, and without any of the institutional support that makes formal recycling viable in wealthier countries.

They are doing the work. They are not receiving the value.

The reason is structural. Without a formal buyer who pays a transparent, consistent, fair price, with documentation, with traceability, with mobile payment, collectors are entirely dependent on aggregators who have every incentive to pay as little as possible. There is no competition for the collector's material. There is no alternative.

What MlimaLoop Changes

MlimaLoop's buying station model is designed from the ground up to change this specific dynamic.

A buying station pays a fixed, published price per kilogram directly to the collector, immediately, at the point of collection. There is no aggregator taking a margin. There is no debt relationship. There is no negotiation on the day based on the aggregator's mood or market position.

At 20kg per day, a collector can already earn more than the majority of Tanzanian waste collectors currently earn. At 35kg per day a collector can earn more than double the typical waste picker's daily income.

The price is the price. It is paid every time. It is documented automatically in the MlimaLoop app and confirmed to the collector via SMS. The collector has a record of every transaction.

And MlimaLoop's tiered collector network model creates something that has never existed in the informal waste economy — a pathway for a collector to earn income from the people they introduce to the system. A collector who recruits others earns a percentage on their direct recruits' collections. A collector who builds a network earns from multiple tiers below them.

The collector who has been working the same route for ten years, who knows every household and market and dumping spot in their area, who has relationships with every other picker in their neighbourhood — that person becomes extraordinarily valuable in MlimaLoop's network. Not just as a collector. As a recruiter. As a route manager. As someone whose local knowledge and relationships are worth paying for systematically.

Dignity Is Not a Side Effect

It would be easy to frame MlimaLoop's approach to collectors as a social impact programme attached to a commercial operation. That framing would be wrong.

The collector network is the feedstock strategy. Reliable, quality-sorted, consistently supplied PET at scale requires a collector network that is fairly paid, well incentivised, and financially stable enough to keep showing up.

Creating dignity in the collection economy is not a side effect of building MlimaLoop. It is the mechanism by which MlimaLoop works.

The people who pick up plastic in East Africa are the first link in a supply chain that ultimately serves some of the world's largest consumer brands. The fleece jacket made from rPET. The food-grade bottle. The certified recycled content that satisfies an ESG commitment or a regulatory requirement.

None of it exists without the collector.

They deserve to be treated accordingly. And paid accordingly. And MlimaLoop is built to make sure they are.
