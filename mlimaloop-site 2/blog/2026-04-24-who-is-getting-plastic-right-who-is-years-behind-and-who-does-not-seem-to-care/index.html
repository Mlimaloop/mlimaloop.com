Insight 24 April 2026

Who Is Getting Plastic Right. Who Is Years Behind. And Who Does Not Seem to Care.

Some countries have cracked the plastic crisis. Their recycling rates are above 90%, their legislation has teeth, and their infrastructure works. Others make pledges and miss targets by decades. And some are not really trying at all. Here is the honest global scorecard.

By MlimaLoop Team
MLIMALOOP

Not every country is losing the plastic crisis at the same rate.

Some have built systems that genuinely work. Where the economics of recovery are so well structured that abandoning plastic is simply irrational. Where collection rates exceed 90%. Where the recycling industry is a mature, profitable, formally employed sector of the economy.

Others have passed legislation, made pledges, set targets, and published roadmaps. And achieved almost nothing. The gap between the announcement and the reality is measured not in percentage points but in decades.

And some countries, producing and consuming plastic at enormous scale, have made no serious attempt to manage it at end of life. The plastic they generate flows into the environment as a matter of course, and the policy response, where one exists at all, is largely cosmetic.

Understanding who has got this right, who is making genuine progress, and who is failing is not an academic exercise. It is the most important policy question of the next thirty years. Because the countries that build the right infrastructure now will have structural economic advantages in the recycling economy for generations. The countries that wait will be importing that infrastructure, and paying a premium for it, long after the window to build it domestically has closed.

Here is the honest global scorecard.

The Countries That Have Got It Right

Norway

Norway's deposit return scheme for plastic bottles is the most successful in the world. Introduced in the 1970s for glass and extended to PET in the 1990s, the system is elegantly simple. Every beverage container sold in Norway carries a deposit. Return it to a machine in any supermarket or petrol station and the deposit is refunded instantly.

PET bottle collection rate: 92% System funding: producer levies, no ongoing government subsidy Key mechanism: economic incentive, not consumer education

Norway did not achieve this through consumer education campaigns. It achieved it by making returning a bottle the economically rational choice. The infrastructure did the work.

Germany

Germany introduced its Pfand deposit return system in 2003 following a crisis in which single-use packaging was proliferating and collection rates were falling. The system covers both single-use and reusable containers and operates through a network of reverse vending machines in virtually every supermarket in the country.

PET bottle collection rate: 97% Framework: one of the world's most comprehensive Extended Producer Responsibility systems Employment: hundreds of thousands in the formal recycling sector

Germany also requires producers to fund the collection and recycling of their packaging from the moment it enters the market. The recycling industry is a formal, well-regulated, economically significant sector.

South Korea

South Korea has achieved recycling rates for plastic packaging above 70% through a combination of mandatory deposit schemes, producer responsibility legislation, and critically, public infrastructure investment in sorting and processing facilities. The country treats recycling as economic infrastructure, not environmental charity. The result is a formal recycling industry with established supply chains, certified output, and direct connections to global manufacturing.

Japan

Japan's approach is different in character but equally effective in outcome. An extraordinarily high cultural emphasis on waste sorting, combined with municipal collection systems that separate waste into as many as ten categories, has produced plastic recycling rates above 80%. Japan also leads in chemical recycling technology development, investing heavily in pyrolysis and gasification systems that can recover value from plastic streams that mechanical recycling cannot handle.

The common thread across all four countries is this. They did not rely on goodwill. They built economic structures that made recovery more rational than disposal. They invested in infrastructure. They created legislation with real consequences for non-compliance. And they sustained those commitments over decades, not electoral cycles.

The Countries Making Serious Progress

Rwanda

Rwanda deserves specific recognition because it has achieved more with less than almost any country on earth.

In 2008, Rwanda introduced one of the world's most comprehensive plastic bag bans, prohibiting the production, import, use, and sale of non-biodegradable plastic bags. Enforcement was genuine, not aspirational. Bags were confiscated at the border. Retailers faced real consequences. The result was a visible, measurable reduction in plastic bag pollution across the country within years of implementation.

Rwanda has since expanded its environmental legislation and is regarded across the continent as the benchmark for what African governments can achieve when political will is matched with enforcement capacity. Kigali is regularly cited as one of the cleanest cities in Africa. That did not happen by accident.

Kenya

Kenya introduced its own plastic bag ban in 2017, one of the strictest in the world, with penalties including fines and imprisonment for carriers, producers, and importers. Implementation has been uneven but the impact on visible plastic bag pollution in Nairobi and other major cities has been measurable.

Kenya also has a growing formal recycling sector, with a number of processor operations collecting and processing PET for export. The informal collector network in Nairobi is one of the largest and most organised in East Africa. The infrastructure is nascent but the direction of travel is clear.

The European Union

The EU as a bloc has moved faster on plastic legislation in the last five years than at any previous point. The Single Use Plastics Directive, which banned the ten most commonly found plastic items on European beaches, came into force in 2021. Mandatory minimum recycled content requirements for PET beverage bottles are being phased in. Extended Producer Responsibility frameworks are being harmonised across member states.

The legislation is real. The targets are binding. The direction is unambiguous. Implementation is uneven across the 27 member states, some are well ahead of the curve, others are scrambling, but the framework is in place and the penalties for non-compliance are real.

The Countries That Talk Loudly and Do Little

This category is crowded. And naming it honestly requires acknowledging that impressive-sounding commitments and genuinely effective policy are very different things.

The United States has no national deposit return scheme. Plastic recycling rates have been falling, not rising, since China stopped accepting contaminated recyclables in 2018. Several states have excellent systems, California's extended producer responsibility framework is genuinely ambitious, but federal policy is fragmented and inconsistent. Voluntary corporate pledges to increase recycled content have largely not been met. The infrastructure to meet them does not exist at the scale required.

Several major plastic-producing nations in Southeast Asia have signed international agreements committing to significant reductions in plastic pollution while simultaneously approving new single-use plastic production capacity. The gap between the treaty commitment and the industrial reality is not measured in years. It is structural.

The UN Global Plastics Treaty, which has been under negotiation since 2022, represents the most ambitious attempt at a binding international framework for plastic pollution. But the negotiations have been repeatedly stalled by producer nations protecting domestic petrochemical industries. The treaty that emerges, if it emerges, is likely to be significantly weaker than what the science demands and what the environmental movement has called for.

Pledges without infrastructure are not policy. They are public relations.

The Countries That Do Not Seem to Care

Some of the world's largest plastic producers and consumers have made no serious attempt to manage plastic at end of life.

Collection infrastructure is absent or overwhelmed. Extended Producer Responsibility frameworks do not exist or are not enforced. Recycling targets, where they exist, have no credible pathway to achievement. The plastic generated flows into the environment as a routine consequence of economic activity.

This is not always a reflection of political indifference. In many cases it reflects the prioritisation of immediate economic development over long-term environmental management, a calculation that wealthier nations made in exactly the same way during their own industrialisation, and which they now ask developing nations not to repeat.

But the trajectory matters enormously. Countries that fail to build waste management infrastructure during their period of fastest economic growth create a legacy of environmental damage that becomes exponentially more expensive to address later. The plastic entering African waterways today will be in the marine environment for five hundred years. The cost of not building infrastructure now is not deferred. It accumulates.

What This Means for East Africa

Tanzania does not yet have a comprehensive Extended Producer Responsibility framework. Formal plastic collection coverage across most cities is below 50%. The recycling industry is almost entirely informal.

But Tanzania also has something that Norway and Germany did not have when they built their systems. It has the benefit of looking at four decades of evidence about what works and what does not. It does not need to experiment. It can build the right infrastructure directly, skipping the wrong turns that wealthier nations took.

Rwanda proved that an African government can implement effective plastic policy when the political will exists. Kenya proved that enforcement is possible. The question for Tanzania is not whether the model exists. It does. The question is when the investment arrives to build the infrastructure that makes the model work economically.

MlimaLoop is not waiting for that question to be answered at the policy level. We are building the infrastructure that creates the economic incentive for collection and recovery regardless of where the policy framework sits today.

Because the countries that got it right did not wait for perfect policy before building infrastructure. They built infrastructure, and the policy followed.
