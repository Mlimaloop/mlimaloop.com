The Plastic Economy Is One of the Biggest on Earth. The Recycling Economy Inside It Is Just Getting Started.

Global plastic production is worth over a trillion dollars a year. It employs tens of millions of people. It is woven into every sector of the modern economy. And sitting inside it, growing faster than almost any industrial sector on the planet, is the recycling economy that will define the next thirty years.

By MlimaLoop Team
MLIMALOOP

Before we talk about recycling, we need to talk about what we are recycling.

Plastic is not a minor industrial material. It is not a niche product category. It is one of the foundational materials of modern civilisation, produced at a scale that most people have never stopped to consider, supporting an economy that touches virtually every sector of global industry.

Understanding the size of the plastic production economy is the first step to understanding the size of the opportunity that sits inside it. Because the recycling economy is not separate from the plastic economy. It is the plastic economy, at the next stage of its evolution.

The Story of Plastic

The first fully synthetic plastic, Bakelite, was invented by Belgian chemist Leo Baekeland in 1907. It was hard, heat-resistant, and could be moulded into any shape — properties that made it immediately valuable for electrical components, consumer goods, and industrial applications.

For the next four decades, plastic development accelerated rapidly. Nylon in 1935. Polyethylene in 1933. Polystyrene in 1930. PVC in the 1920s. Each new polymer opened new applications and new markets. By the end of the Second World War, plastic had moved from a curiosity to a strategic industrial material.

The post-war period saw the explosion. Mass consumer culture, rising incomes, and the search for lightweight, cheap, durable materials drove plastic production from approximately 2 million tonnes per year in 1950 to 15 million tonnes by 1964. Then 50 million tonnes by 1976. Then 100 million tonnes by 1989.

The curve has never bent downward.

Global plastic production: 1950 — 2 million tonnes per year 1989 — 100 million tonnes per year 2000 — 213 million tonnes per year 2023 — 430 million tonnes per year 2040 projection — 700+ million tonnes per year Total ever produced — approximately 9.2 billion tonnes

In total, since Baekeland created Bakelite in 1907, humanity has produced an estimated 9.2 billion tonnes of plastic. That is more than one tonne of plastic for every person alive on earth today. Produced, used, and in 79% of cases disposed of into landfill or the natural environment.

The Economy Plastic Supports

The global plastic industry is worth approximately 600 billion dollars per year in direct production value. When downstream industries that depend on plastic — packaging, automotive, construction, electronics, textiles, medical devices, agriculture — are included, the figure rises significantly higher.

Plastic packaging alone is a market worth over 400 billion dollars annually. The automotive industry uses approximately 150 kg of plastic per vehicle. The construction industry is the second largest consumer of plastic globally, using it for pipes, insulation, window frames, and flooring. The medical industry depends on plastic for virtually every sterile device, IV bag, and disposable component that keeps modern healthcare functioning.

The plastic industry directly employs an estimated 1.5 million people in manufacturing in Europe alone. In the United States, the plastics and rubber products sector employs over 900,000 people. Globally, including the full value chain, the number of people whose livelihoods depend directly on the plastic economy runs into the tens of millions.

This is not an industry that can be wished away. It is structural infrastructure of the global economy, and it will remain so for the foreseeable future.

The question is not whether plastic will be produced. It is what happens to it when it is no longer needed.

The Recycling Economy: From Cottage to Corporation

The recycling economy is not new. It is ancient.

Humans have recycled metals since the Bronze Age. Glass and paper recycling industries existed in pre-industrial Europe. Rag and bone merchants, collecting materials for reprocessing, were a feature of every major city in the nineteenth century.

Plastic recycling, as a formal industry, is younger. The first large-scale mechanical recycling of post-consumer plastic began in the 1970s. Deposit return schemes in Scandinavia, which drove high collection rates for glass and then PET, created the first reliable feedstock streams. The 1988 introduction of resin identification codes gave the informal and formal sorting industries a common language.

The global plastic recycling market today: Current value — approximately 57 billion dollars per year Projected value by 2030 — 100 billion dollars Projected value by 2035 — 150 billion dollars Growth rate — one of the fastest growing industrial sectors on the planet

But the industry spans an extraordinary range of scales, from the most informal to the most sophisticated.

At one end, the informal collector in Arusha or Nairobi or Lagos, working with no equipment, no protection, and no formal recognition, recovering post-consumer plastic with their hands and selling it to whoever is buying. This person is part of the recycling economy. An essential part. The feedstock they collect has value that eventually reaches global supply chains, even if almost none of that value reaches them.

Above them, the aggregators who consolidate material, provide capital, and create the link between individual collectors and processing facilities. Informal, largely ungoverned, but performing a genuine logistics function.

Then the processors — from small-scale community operations producing low-grade rPET granules for local manufacturing, through to mid-scale facilities like MlimaLoop producing certified rPET pellet for global export, through to the large industrial operations in Europe, Asia, and North America processing hundreds of thousands of tonnes per year.

And at the top end, the publicly listed recycled materials companies, the commodity traders dealing in certified rPET flake and pellet on international markets, the chemical recycling companies backed by venture capital and strategic investment from the world's largest consumer goods corporations.

All of it is the recycling economy. All of it is growing.

The Jobs and Income Story

The economic case for building the recycling industry is not only about material value. It is about employment.

Globally, the waste and recycling sector employs an estimated 15 to 20 million people. The vast majority are in informal roles in developing economies. They are the collectors, the sorters, the aggregators who form the foundation of the recycling supply chain in Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

The formalisation of this workforce — turning informal, unprotected, poorly paid roles into documented, fairly compensated, formally employed positions — is one of the most significant economic development opportunities available in East Africa right now.

Consider the mathematics. A MlimaLoop buying station pays $0.14 per kilogram to every collector, immediately, with documentation. An informal aggregator pays $0.07 to $0.11 per kilogram, with no documentation and no certainty. The difference, for a collector gathering 25 kilograms per day, is the difference between $1.75 to $2.75 per day and $3.50 per day — more than doubling the income of some of the most economically marginalised workers in the regional economy.

Scale that across a network of 200 collectors, then 2,000, then 20,000 — which is the scale that a regional rPET processing operation ultimately requires — and the income creation is significant. Not transformative in isolation, but substantial when combined with the upstream job creation in facility operations, quality control, logistics, administration, and management.

A mid-scale rPET processing facility employs directly between 30 and 80 people in operational roles. It supports indirectly the livelihoods of every collector in its network. It generates tax revenue. It creates skills and training pathways. It connects a local economy to global supply chains that would otherwise have no presence in the region.

This is what formal recycling infrastructure creates. Not just environmental benefit. Economic infrastructure.

The Future of the Planet Depends on Getting This Right

Here is the economic case for urgency that does not rely on environmental guilt.

The brands that are legally required to include minimum percentages of recycled content in their packaging — under EU legislation, UK tax frameworks, and voluntary commitments that are becoming contractual obligations to institutional investors — need supply. Certified, traceable, specification-grade supply. And they cannot get enough of it.

The global certified rPET market is in structural deficit. Demand from brand owners, driven by legislation and ESG commitments, is growing faster than supply from recycling processors. The premium for certified material over virgin PET is significant and growing. The processor who can supply GRS-certified rPET into this market, with full chain of custody documentation and independent quality verification, is selling into a seller's market.

In East Africa, that supply is currently zero. Not because the feedstock does not exist. It does, in enormous volume. Not because the technology does not exist. It does, and it is accessible. Not because the demand does not exist. It is active and growing. But because the infrastructure to connect feedstock to technology to demand has never been built.

MlimaLoop is building it.

We are not a charity operating at the edge of the plastic economy. We are not a well-intentioned community project making colourful bricks. We are building industrial infrastructure at the frontier of one of the most important economic transitions of the next thirty years.

The transition from a linear economy, in which plastic is produced, used, and discarded, to a circular one, in which plastic is produced, used, recovered, and reprocessed back into production, is not optional. Legislation is mandating it. Brand commitments are requiring it. Institutional investors are pricing it. Consumer preference is rewarding it.

The question is not whether that transition happens. It is happening. The question is who builds the infrastructure that captures the value of it in East Africa.

The plastic economy is one of the biggest on earth. The recycling economy inside it is just getting started.

And we are building our piece of it. Right now. In Arusha.
