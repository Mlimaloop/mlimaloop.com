The Supply Chain Nobody Talks About: A Plastic Bottle's Journey From Oil Field to Recycled Content

Every plastic bottle starts as crude oil and ends as either waste or recycled material. Between those two points is one of the most complex and least understood supply chains in the global economy. Here is the full journey — and where MlimaLoop fits into it.

By MlimaLoop Team
MLIMALOOP

Pick up a plastic water bottle and hold it for a moment.

What you are holding started its life as crude oil, extracted from a well somewhere in the Middle East, the United States, Russia, or one of the other major petroleum-producing regions of the world. It has travelled through a refinery, a petrochemical plant, a bottle manufacturing facility, a filling plant, a distribution network, a retailer, and the hands of a consumer before it reached you.

And when you are done with it, it will either travel through another supply chain, one that is less understood, less documented, and far less funded than the one that created it, and emerge as certified recycled material for a global brand's packaging, or it will enter the environment and stay there for five hundred years.

The supply chain that creates plastic bottles is one of the most sophisticated industrial systems ever built. The supply chain that recovers them is, in most of the world, barely a supply chain at all.

Here is the full journey. Every stage. And where MlimaLoop enters it.

Stage One: The Oil Field

A plastic water bottle begins as crude oil. Specifically, the naphtha fraction produced during oil refining, a light hydrocarbon stream that forms the feedstock for the petrochemical industry.

At a steam cracker, naphtha is heated to extremely high temperatures in the presence of steam, breaking the large hydrocarbon molecules into smaller ones. The products of this cracking process include ethylene and paraxylene, the two precursor chemicals that eventually become the building blocks of PET.

The oil fields that supply this feedstock are distributed across the globe. Saudi Arabia. The United States. Russia. The UAE. Iraq. Kuwait. The geopolitical complexity of the petroleum supply chain is embedded in every plastic bottle before it is even manufactured. When oil prices rise due to conflict, sanctions, or production decisions made by OPEC, the price of virgin PET follows.

Stage Two: The Petrochemical Plant

Ethylene, produced at the steam cracker, is oxidised and reacted with acetic acid to produce ethylene glycol, one of the two building blocks of PET. Paraxylene, also derived from naphtha, is oxidised to produce purified terephthalic acid, the other building block.

At a PET polymerisation plant, purified terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol are combined under heat and pressure in the presence of a catalyst. The condensation reaction that results links the two molecules together repeatedly, releasing water and forming the long polymer chains that give PET its properties. The resulting material, PET resin, is pelletised and shipped to bottle manufacturers around the world.

Global PET resin production exceeds 80 million tonnes per year. The major production hubs are China, India, the United States, and several Middle Eastern countries with access to cheap petrochemical feedstocks.

Stage Three: The Bottle Manufacturer

PET resin pellets are shipped to a bottle manufacturing facility where they are processed through an injection stretch blow moulding machine. The pellets are melted, injected into a mould to form a preform, a test-tube shaped intermediate, which is then reheated and blown into the final bottle shape using high-pressure air.

Modern bottle manufacturing is extraordinarily precise and fast. A single production line can produce tens of thousands of bottles per hour, to tolerances measured in fractions of a millimetre. The wall thickness, the neck finish, the base geometry, and the thread profile are all controlled to specifications that ensure compatibility with filling equipment, caps, and labels designed by different manufacturers in different countries.

Stage Four: The Filling Plant

At the filling plant, bottles are taken from flat-packed storage, erected, filled with water or beverage, capped, labelled, and packed for distribution. For carbonated drinks, the filling process must maintain precise pressure to prevent carbonation loss. For water, hygiene standards require sterile filling conditions.

The filling plant is where the supply chain that creates the product meets the consumer. It is also, typically, where the producer's legal and financial relationship with the bottle ends. Once the bottle leaves the filling plant, responsibility for its end of life, in most markets, belongs to someone else.

Stage Five: Distribution and Retail

Filled bottles move through a distribution network involving trucking, warehousing, and in some cases ocean freight for imported brands. They arrive at retailers, from large supermarkets to small kiosks, and are sold to consumers.

In East Africa, this distribution network is extensive and well-developed. The bottle that was manufactured from petrochemical feedstocks and filled at an industrial facility in Dar es Salaam or Nairobi reaches a consumer in a small town in northern Tanzania within days.

Stage Six: The Consumer

The consumer uses the bottle for its intended purpose and then faces a decision that, in most of East Africa, has only one practical outcome.

There is no deposit to recover. There is no formal collection infrastructure. There is no financial incentive to retain the bottle and seek out a collection point. The bottle is discarded.

Where it goes from here depends entirely on the informal systems that exist around the consumer. In a city with even minimal informal collection activity, there is a reasonable chance a collector will find the bottle and recover it. In a rural area with no collectors and no collection point, the bottle enters the environment directly.

Stage Seven: The Informal Collector

In markets with informal recycling economies, the bottle's recovery from the environment depends on an individual collector who picks it up, carries it to an aggregator or buying station, and receives a payment that has historically been between seven and eleven cents per kilogram of material.

For the bottle that is collected, this is where the recovery supply chain begins. For the bottle that is not collected, this is where the journey ends. In the soil, the drainage channel, the river, or the ocean. For five hundred years.

The collector is the most essential and least recognised participant in the entire supply chain that connects a petroleum field in the Middle East to a recycled content commitment in a European brand's sustainability report.

Stage Eight: The Aggregator

Collected bottles move from individual collectors to aggregators who consolidate material from multiple sources, provide storage, and sell in volume to processors or exporters. In East Africa, this aggregation layer is almost entirely informal. No contracts. No price transparency. No documentation of origin.

The aggregator captures a margin between the price paid to collectors and the price received from processors. In a market without formal infrastructure, this margin is the aggregator's entire value proposition and there is nothing to constrain it.

Stage Nine: The Processor

At a processing facility, collected PET bottles are sorted, granulated, washed, dried, and extruded into pellets. The quality of the output depends on the quality of the input, the sophistication of the washing and sorting process, and the specification of the extrusion equipment.

In most of East Africa, there is no certified processing facility. Material collected by informal networks is either exported as low-value baled scrap or processed into granules without any certification or quality documentation.

This is the gap. A bottle collected in Arusha today cannot enter a certified supply chain because there is no certified processing facility to receive it.

This is exactly where MlimaLoop enters the supply chain.

Stage Ten: MlimaLoop

A collector arrives at a MlimaLoop buying station with a load of post-consumer PET bottles. The load is weighed, documented in the MlimaLoop app, and the collector is paid immediately at a fixed, published price of $0.14 per kilogram.

From this moment, every kilogram is traceable. The collector, the weight, the location, and the time are recorded against a batch number that follows the material through every subsequent stage of processing.

The bottles are transported to MlimaLoop's facility in Arusha, sorted, granulated, hot-washed at 85 to 90 degrees Celsius with caustic solution, dried to below 50 parts per million moisture, and extruded into uniform pellets. For food-grade output, the pellets pass through the SSP reactor, rebuilding molecular chain length to food-contact specification.

Every batch is tested internally and by accredited external laboratories. Every batch is associated with the chain of custody documentation from collector intake. Every batch is GRS certified.

Stage Eleven: The Brand

A global brand purchases GRS-certified rPET pellets from MlimaLoop. The pellets are incorporated into its packaging production at a percentage that meets the brand's voluntary commitments and the minimum recycled content requirements of the EU or UK markets into which the product is sold.

The brand can demonstrate to regulators, to institutional investors, and to consumers that its recycled content claim is genuine, independently verified, and traceable to a specific collecting community in Arusha, Tanzania.

The bottle that started as crude oil in an oil field, passed through a petrochemical plant, a bottle manufacturer, a filling facility, a retailer, a consumer, an informal collector, and a certified processing facility, is now certified recycled content in a brand's global packaging.

The loop is closed.

Why This Supply Chain Matters

The supply chain that creates plastic bottles is worth hundreds of billions of dollars annually. It is sophisticated, well-funded, and supported by decades of industrial infrastructure investment.

The supply chain that recovers them, particularly in East Africa, is worth a fraction of that. It is fragmented, informal, underfunded, and missing its most critical component: the certified industrial processing facility that connects recovered material to global demand.

MlimaLoop is building that component. Not as a charitable intervention at the margin of the plastic economy. As the industrial infrastructure that completes a supply chain that currently ends in the environment.

The bottle in your hand has a story that runs from an oil field to a recycling facility and back into a brand's packaging. In most of the world, that story is only half told. In East Africa, it is barely started.

We are writing the second half.
