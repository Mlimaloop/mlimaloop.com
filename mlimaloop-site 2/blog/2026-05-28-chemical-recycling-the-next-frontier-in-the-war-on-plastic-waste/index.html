Chemical Recycling: The Next Frontier in the War on Plastic Waste

Mechanical recycling handles the clean, sorted, high-quality plastic that MlimaLoop processes. But what about everything else? The contaminated, the mixed, the unrecyclable fraction? Chemical recycling is the answer — and it is maturing faster than most people realise. Here is what it is, how it works, and why it matters.

By MlimaLoop Team
MLIMALOOP

There is a limit to what mechanical recycling can do.

It is a generous limit. The hot wash process, the granulator, the extruder, the SSP reactor — together they can convert clean, sorted, post-consumer PET into certified recycled material that meets the most demanding food-contact specifications in the world. MlimaLoop's facility is built around this process because it works, it is proven, and the output has established global markets.

But mechanical recycling works best on clean, sorted, single-polymer material. Mixed plastics. Contaminated films. Multi-layer composite packaging. The plastic that has degraded beyond the point where its intrinsic viscosity can be restored. These materials resist the mechanical process. They contaminate the output stream. They reduce yield and quality in ways that make them uneconomical to include.

In the past, the answer to this problem was simple and environmentally damaging. Landfill it. Burn it. Export it to countries with lower environmental standards and hope someone else would deal with it.

Chemical recycling is a different answer. And it is maturing into a commercially viable technology faster than most people in the mainstream recycling conversation have registered.

What Chemical Recycling Is

Chemical recycling is a broad term covering several distinct technologies that share a common principle. Rather than melting and reforming plastic as a physical material, as mechanical recycling does, chemical recycling breaks plastic down at the molecular level, converting it back into its chemical building blocks or into synthetic fuels and gases.

The output is not recycled plastic pellet. It is chemical feedstock, fuel, or gas that can be used to manufacture new plastic or to generate energy. The process works on mixed, contaminated, and degraded plastic streams that mechanical recycling cannot handle.

There are three main chemical recycling technologies in commercial or near-commercial development.

Pyrolysis Temperature: 400 to 700 degrees Celsius, no oxygen Output: pyrolysis oil — refined into synthetic fuel or plastic feedstock Best for: mixed, contaminated plastic streams Commercial status: most advanced, multiple commercial facilities operating globally

Pyrolysis heats plastic waste in the absence of oxygen to temperatures between 400 and 700 degrees Celsius. Without oxygen, the plastic cannot combust. Instead, the polymer chains break down into shorter hydrocarbon molecules, producing a pyrolysis oil that can be refined into synthetic fuel or used as a feedstock for new plastic manufacturing. Pyrolysis works on mixed plastic streams and is relatively tolerant of contamination. It is the most commercially advanced of the chemical recycling technologies.

Gasification Temperature: very high, with controlled oxygen or steam Output: syngas (carbon monoxide and hydrogen) Best for: very large-scale mixed waste operations Commercial status: operating at scale, capital intensive

Gasification converts plastic waste into a synthetic gas called syngas, a mixture of carbon monoxide and hydrogen, by heating it at very high temperatures with a controlled amount of oxygen or steam. Syngas can be used directly as a fuel or as a chemical feedstock for manufacturing processes including the production of new plastics, chemicals, and synthetic fuels.

Depolymerisation Method: chemical reactions (glycolysis, hydrolysis, methanolysis) Output: virgin-equivalent monomers (for PET: purified terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol) Best for: specific polymers including PET Commercial status: significant investment from BASF, Eastman, Indorama — approaching commercial scale

Depolymerisation takes a more targeted approach, breaking specific polymers back into their monomer building blocks through chemical reactions rather than thermal processes. For PET specifically, depolymerisation processes can convert post-consumer PET back into purified terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol, the same virgin feedstocks used to manufacture new PET. The output is chemically identical to virgin material and can be used to produce food-contact PET without the limitations of mechanical recycling.

Why Chemical Recycling Matters

The significance of chemical recycling is not that it replaces mechanical recycling. It is that it extends the recovery economy to the materials that mechanical recycling leaves behind.

In a world with only mechanical recycling, a meaningful fraction of plastic waste has no recovery pathway. Multi-layer packaging is unrecyclable by mechanical means because the different layers cannot be separated. Contaminated plastic cannot be cleaned to the standard required for quality output. Plastic that has degraded significantly in the environment may have lost the structural integrity needed for extrusion. Black plastic is invisible to optical sorting systems. Mixed plastic streams produce low-quality output with limited market value.

All of these material streams have significant embedded energy. They represent petroleum feedstocks that were extracted, processed, and manufactured into products. That value has not disappeared because the plastic has become contaminated or mixed or degraded. Chemical recycling provides the process to recover it.

Together, mechanical and chemical recycling create the conditions for a genuinely complete plastic recovery economy. Every piece of plastic that is collected has a destination. Every destination captures value. None of it needs to be abandoned.

The Investment Landscape

Chemical recycling has attracted significant investment from both the private sector and major industrial corporations in recent years, reflecting growing confidence that the technology is approaching commercial viability at meaningful scale.

Eastman Chemical Company has committed over one billion dollars to a molecular recycling facility in France that will use methanolysis to convert post-consumer PET into virgin-equivalent feedstock. The facility is expected to process 160,000 tonnes of plastic waste per year when fully operational.

BASF has invested in its ChemCycling programme, using pyrolysis oil derived from mixed plastic waste as a feedstock for its chemical manufacturing processes, producing certified circular plastics for brand customers.

Plastic Energy, a UK-based pyrolysis operator, has operating facilities in Spain and the UK and has announced partnerships with major brands including Nestlé, Unilever, and TotalEnergies. The company's technology processes mixed plastic waste including film, flexible packaging, and contaminated streams that mechanical recycling cannot handle.

The common thread across these investments is the recognition that chemical recycling is not a competitor to mechanical recycling. It is a complementary technology that extends the reach of the recycling economy into material streams that mechanical processes leave behind.

What Chemical Recycling Means for East Africa

Chemical recycling is not yet part of MlimaLoop's operational scope. The priority for the Arusha pilot facility is to build, certify, and scale the mechanical rPET processing operation that represents the most commercially immediate opportunity.

But the trajectory of chemical recycling technology is directly relevant to MlimaLoop's long-term development for two reasons.

First, as MlimaLoop's collector network grows, it will recover not just clean PET but the full range of plastic waste present in the Arusha urban environment. HDPE, PP, contaminated film, mixed plastic streams. The mechanical facility handles PET. The other materials currently have limited local recovery options. As chemical recycling technology matures and becomes more accessible at smaller scale, it extends the value of the collector network MlimaLoop is building to material streams that currently have no formal destination.

Second, the depolymerisation of PET is directly relevant to the premium end of MlimaLoop's market. Depolymerisation produces virgin-equivalent PET feedstock that satisfies the most demanding brand requirements and regulatory standards. As the technology becomes commercially accessible at smaller scale, it represents a potential upgrade pathway for MlimaLoop's processing output that would command the highest possible market prices.

The Complete Picture

The plastic recovery economy, fully developed, looks like this.

Clean, sorted, high-quality PET is processed mechanically into certified rPET pellet for packaging and fibre applications. MlimaLoop's core operation today.

End-of-life and degraded PET is processed through depolymerisation into virgin-equivalent feedstock. The same output, different input quality.

Mixed, contaminated, multi-layer plastic that cannot be mechanically or chemically recycled into plastic feedstock is processed through pyrolysis or gasification into synthetic fuel or energy. Zero plastic to landfill or environment.

The collector network that feeds all three pathways is the same network. The buying stations are the same buying stations. The economic incentive for collection is the same economic incentive. What changes is the destination of the material based on its quality and composition.

This is the complete circular plastic economy. Not a distant vision. A direction of travel that the technology, the investment, and the regulatory environment are all pointing toward simultaneously.

MlimaLoop is building the first stage of it. In Arusha. With the collector network, the certification infrastructure, and the operational knowledge that the complete picture will eventually require.

The next frontier is already being built. We are starting at the foundation.
