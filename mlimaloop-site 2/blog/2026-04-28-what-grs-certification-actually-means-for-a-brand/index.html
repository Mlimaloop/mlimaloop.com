What GRS Certification Actually Means for a Brand — And Why East Africa Is the Gap in Their Supply Chain

Global brands have made recycled content commitments they cannot currently meet. The certification they need is GRS. The supply they cannot source is certified rPET from East Africa. Here is what GRS actually demands, why brands need it, and why the gap MlimaLoop fills is worth billions.

By MlimaLoop Team
MLIMALOOP

Somewhere in the sustainability department of almost every major consumer goods brand in the world, there is a spreadsheet.

It lists the recycled content commitments the brand has made publicly. The percentage of recycled material in their packaging by 2025. By 2027. By 2030. The targets announced at press conferences, written into annual reports, communicated to institutional investors as evidence of ESG seriousness.

And next to those targets, in a column that does not appear in the press release, is the current status. How much certified recycled content they have actually been able to source. How far short of the commitment they currently are. How many tonnes of GRS-certified rPET they need that they cannot find a supplier for.

For many brands, that gap is significant. For brands operating in East Africa, it is total. There is no certified rPET supply chain in the region. Every bottle they sell into the Tanzanian, Kenyan, or Ugandan market contributes to a plastic footprint that cannot currently be offset by in-market recycling because the infrastructure to do it does not exist.

That is the gap MlimaLoop fills. But to understand why it matters commercially, you need to understand what GRS certification actually is and what it demands from a supply chain.

What GRS Certification Is

The Global Recycled Standard is an international, voluntary standard that sets requirements for third-party certification of recycled content in products and supply chains. It was developed by Control Union Certifications and is now administered by Textile Exchange, one of the world's leading textile sustainability organisations.

GRS certification applies to any product that contains recycled material — textiles, packaging, plastics, metals, paper. It covers the full supply chain from the point of recycling through to the final product, and it requires independent third-party auditing at every stage.

GRS certification requires: 1. Accurate, verifiable recycled content claims with auditable chain of custody 2. Independent third-party auditing at every stage of the supply chain 3. Minimum social and environmental standards at every certified facility 4. Unbroken documentation from collector intake to finished product

GRS certification is not a self-declaration. A brand cannot simply state that it uses recycled content and claim GRS compliance. Every facility in the supply chain must be independently audited and certified by an accredited Certification Body. The chain of custody documentation must be maintained and auditable from collector intake through to finished product.

This is why GRS certification is genuinely meaningful and why brands that require it from their suppliers are making a substantive demand rather than a cosmetic one.

Why Brands Need It

The reasons a brand needs GRS-certified supply rather than uncertified recycled content are multiple and increasingly non-negotiable.

Regulatory compliance. The EU's Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation requires minimum recycled content in PET beverage bottles, with percentages rising through the decade. Demonstrating compliance requires certified, auditable chain of custody documentation. Uncertified claims do not satisfy the regulatory requirement.

Investor and reporting obligations. Institutional investors and ESG rating agencies increasingly require brands to demonstrate their sustainability claims with third-party verified data. A recycled content claim without GRS certification or equivalent third-party verification is not credible for reporting purposes under frameworks like the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive.

Consumer trust. The greenwashing enforcement environment is tightening significantly. The EU's Green Claims Directive, which came into force in 2024, prohibits sustainability claims that cannot be substantiated with independent verification. A brand that claims recycled content without certification faces legal exposure in regulated markets.

Supply chain transparency. Major retailers are increasingly requiring their suppliers to demonstrate certified sustainable sourcing. A brand that cannot produce GRS certificates for its recycled content claims risks losing shelf space in premium retail channels that have adopted supplier sustainability requirements.

The cumulative effect of these pressures is that GRS certification has moved from a nice-to-have to a non-negotiable requirement for brands operating in regulated markets with serious sustainability commitments.

What the Supply Chain Actually Has to Prove

This is where the commercial case for certified processors becomes concrete.

For a brand to make a GRS-compliant recycled content claim, every facility that handles the material from collection through to finished product must be GRS certified. That means the washing and processing facility. The extruder. The SSP reactor if food-grade material is being produced. The pellet packer and shipper.

It also means the chain of custody must be unbroken and auditable. The brand must be able to demonstrate, from the GRS transaction certificates it holds, that the recycled content in its product came from certified recycled material processed through certified facilities.

This is why a certified rPET processor is not interchangeable with an uncertified one. The brand's certification depends on the processor's certification. A brand that sources rPET from an uncertified processor in East Africa cannot make a GRS-compliant recycled content claim, regardless of how well-intentioned the processor's operation is.

MlimaLoop's facility is being designed and built to GRS certification standards from day one. The chain of custody system, the collector intake documentation, the batch records, the quality management system, the social and environmental standards for the facility and the collector network are all being built to satisfy the GRS audit requirements before the first production run.

This is not an afterthought. It is the foundation. Because without GRS certification, MlimaLoop's output cannot serve the market that needs it most.

East Africa: The Gap That Brands Cannot Bridge

Here is the commercial reality that makes MlimaLoop's position so significant.

The world's largest beverage brands have all made public commitments to recycled content in their packaging. They all sell into East African markets. They are all, to varying degrees, unable to source certified rPET from within those markets because no certified processing facility exists.

The situation for global brands in East Africa: Environmental failure: bottles sold into East African markets are not being recycled in-market Compliance risk: EPR legislation arriving will create regulatory liability Commercial opportunity: first certified processor has a structural supply chain advantage that is very hard to displace

It is a commercial opportunity because the first certified rPET processor in the region that can supply these brands with documented, auditable, GRS-certified recycled content from their own market will have a structural supply chain relationship that is very difficult for a later entrant to displace.

That is not a speculative future opportunity. The brands are already looking. The demand already exists. The question is whether the supply will be there when the regulatory pressure makes it non-negotiable.

The Brands That Get Ahead of This Win

There is a difference between a brand that builds a supply chain partnership with a certified processor before EPR legislation requires it and a brand that waits until it has no choice.

The brand that partners early gets pricing negotiated before scarcity drives premiums higher. It gets supply security when other brands are competing for limited certified output. It gets the reputational benefit of demonstrating proactive supply chain development rather than reactive compliance scrambling. And it gets the data and chain of custody documentation it needs for its ESG reporting and investor communications well ahead of the mandatory reporting deadlines.

The brand that waits gets none of those advantages. It pays more. It competes for limited supply. And it reports compliance retrospectively rather than proactively.
