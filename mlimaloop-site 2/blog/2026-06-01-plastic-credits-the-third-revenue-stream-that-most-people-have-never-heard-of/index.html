Plastic Credits: The Third Revenue Stream That Most People Have Never Heard Of

Most people understand that MlimaLoop sells certified rPET pellet. Fewer people know about plastic credits — the third revenue stream that turns every kilogram collected into a tradeable environmental asset. Here is what plastic credits are, how the market works, who buys them, and why they matter for MlimaLoop's commercial model.

By MlimaLoop Team
MLIMALOOP

There is a carbon credit market. Most people have heard of it. Companies with unavoidable emissions buy credits generated by projects that remove or avoid carbon dioxide — reforestation, renewable energy, methane capture — to offset their footprint.

The plastic credit market works on the same principle. Companies that produce or use plastic packaging buy credits generated by projects that collect and process plastic waste — particularly in regions where that plastic would otherwise enter the environment. The credit represents a verified, independently audited tonne of plastic recovered from an at-risk environment.

The market is younger than carbon credits. It is less standardised. It is growing faster.

And for MlimaLoop, it represents a third revenue stream, alongside rPET pellet sales and recycled fibre, that generates income from the same collection activity that produces the certified material. Every kilogram collected at a buying station in Arusha is simultaneously feedstock for the processing facility and a potential plastic credit for a brand seeking to offset its plastic footprint.

What Plastic Credits Are

A plastic credit is a certificate representing the collection and verified recovery of a specific weight of plastic waste from an at-risk environment. One plastic credit typically represents one tonne of plastic recovered, documented, and independently audited.

The credit is generated by the organisation that collects and processes the plastic, verified by an independent certification body, and issued on a recognised registry. Once issued, it can be sold to companies or organisations seeking to offset their plastic footprint, demonstrate environmental responsibility, or meet voluntary sustainability commitments.

The key word is verified. A plastic credit is not a self-declaration that plastic has been collected. It requires independent auditing of the collection records, the chain of custody from collector to processor, the weight of material recovered, and the geographic and social context of the collection. The verification process is what gives the credit value. Without it, the claim is marketing. With it, it is a tradeable environmental asset.

The Ocean Bound Plastic Premium

Not all plastic credits are equal. The most valuable category is ocean bound plastic credits.

Ocean bound plastic is defined as plastic waste collected within fifty kilometres of a coastline, or within the catchment of a waterway that flows to the sea, in an area where waste management infrastructure is inadequate. Material collected in these zones is assessed as being at high risk of entering the ocean if not recovered.

For buyers, ocean bound plastic credits carry a higher environmental value than standard plastic credits because they represent the prevention of marine plastic pollution, which is both more damaging and more visible than terrestrial plastic disposal. MlimaLoop's Arusha collection operation qualifies for ocean bound plastic certification under the Zero Plastic Oceans standard.

This is not a technicality. It is a genuine environmental reality. The plastic that MlimaLoop collects in Arusha would, in the absence of the buying station network, have a realistic pathway to the Indian Ocean via the Pangani system. The credit issued for its collection is a verified statement that this pathway has been interrupted.

Who Buys Plastic Credits and Why

The buyers of plastic credits are primarily companies with plastic footprints they cannot immediately eliminate through product redesign or material substitution, and sustainability commitments that require them to demonstrate action on those footprints.

Consumer goods brands with plastic packaging commitments. A beverage brand that has committed to reducing its plastic footprint but cannot yet reformulate all its packaging can purchase plastic credits to offset the gap between its current footprint and its commitment. The credit provides independently verified evidence of plastic recovery for sustainability reporting.

Retailers and e-commerce companies with packaging sustainability targets. The growth of e-commerce has created enormous volumes of single-use plastic packaging. Retailers facing pressure from institutional investors and consumers to address their packaging footprint are active buyers of plastic credits.

Financial institutions and asset managers with ESG commitments. Investors seeking to demonstrate the environmental credentials of their portfolios are purchasing plastic credits on behalf of portfolio companies or as direct ESG investments.

Events, venues, and hospitality businesses. Conferences, festivals, hotels, and restaurants generating single-use plastic in their operations purchase credits to offset that footprint, often as part of broader sustainability certification programmes.

Airlines and travel companies. The travel sector generates significant single-use plastic in in-flight service, airport operations, and accommodation. Several major airlines have purchased plastic credits as part of their sustainability programmes.

How the Market Works

The plastic credit market operates through several registries and standards bodies that provide the verification, certification, and trading infrastructure.

Verra, the organisation that administers the Verified Carbon Standard for carbon credits, has developed the Plastic Waste Reduction Standard, which provides a framework for verifying and certifying plastic credit projects. Projects registered under the Verra standard undergo independent auditing by accredited verifiers and issue credits on Verra's publicly accessible registry.

Zero Plastic Oceans administers the ocean bound plastic certification standard, providing specific certification for plastic collected from ocean bound sources. The OBP certification is recognised by major brands and by the Verra standard.

South Pole, EcoAct, ClimatePartner, and several specialist plastic credit brokers operate in the market as intermediaries, connecting project developers with buyers and providing price discovery, contract structuring, and transaction facilitation services.

What This Means for MlimaLoop

For MlimaLoop, plastic credits are not a separate programme attached to the recycling operation. They are a natural output of the same collection and documentation system that supports GRS certification and rPET pellet sales.

Every kilogram collected at a MlimaLoop buying station is documented in the MlimaLoop app with collector identity, weight, location, time, and GPS coordinates. This documentation is the foundation of the chain of custody system required for GRS certification. It is also the collection record required for Verra plastic credit verification and Zero Plastic Oceans OBP certification.

The same data. The same documentation system. Three revenue streams.

Plastic credit revenue is therefore not a marginal addition to MlimaLoop's financial model. At meaningful collection volumes it is a significant and growing income stream that improves the economics of collection at the buying station level, making it possible to pay collectors a higher price per kilogram while maintaining the overall financial viability of the operation.

As the plastic credit market matures and pricing strengthens, driven by growing corporate demand and the development of regulatory frameworks that recognise plastic credit purchases as part of extended producer responsibility compliance, this revenue stream becomes progressively more valuable.

The Broader Market Trajectory

The plastic credit market is at an earlier stage of development than the carbon credit market, which itself has faced significant credibility and standardisation challenges over the past decade. The lessons from carbon markets, around the importance of independent verification, additionality, permanence, and avoiding double counting, are being actively applied to plastic credit market design.

The result is a market that is smaller, less liquid, and less standardised than carbon credits, but that is learning from its predecessor's mistakes rather than repeating them. The major standards bodies are establishing rigorous verification requirements. Independent auditing is built into the certification process from the start. The chain of custody requirements that MlimaLoop's documentation system satisfies are specifically designed to prevent the credibility failures that have damaged parts of the carbon credit market.

The trajectory is toward a market where verified, high-quality plastic credits from certified collection operations in ocean bound source geographies trade at prices that reflect their genuine environmental value. MlimaLoop's Arusha operation, with its GRS certification, its ocean bound plastic status, and its full chain of custody documentation from collector to processed output, is positioned to produce the highest quality credits in the market.

The loop is not just circular. It is financially complete.
