Plastic on Land: How a Silent Crisis is Destroying Africa's Wildlife and Ecosystems

From elephants with stomachs full of plastic to livestock dying in their thousands, the land-based plastic crisis across Africa is accelerating. Here is the scale of what is happening — and why it cannot wait.

By MlimaLoop Team
MLIMALOOP

In 2024, a wildlife ranger in Kenya reported something that stopped him in his tracks. An elephant — one of the most intelligent and ecologically vital animals on the planet — had charged and injured a visitor near a waste dump on the edge of Amboseli National Park. The post-mortem told the real story. The elephant's stomach contained kilograms of plastic waste. Bags, packaging, bottle caps. Material it had ingested while foraging near a landfill that had encroached on its natural feeding ground.

It was not an isolated incident. It was a symptom.

Across Africa, plastic waste on land is no longer a litter problem. It is an ecological emergency — one that is accelerating faster than most people realise, and one whose consequences extend far beyond the animals that cannot avoid it.

The Scale of the Problem

Africa generates an estimated 17 million tonnes of plastic waste every year. Less than 4% is formally recycled. The rest — the overwhelming majority — is burned, buried, dumped in open sites, or left to accumulate in communities, waterways, and wild spaces.

In East Africa alone, the figures are staggering. Tanzania, Kenya, Uganda, and Ethiopia collectively produce millions of tonnes of plastic annually. Municipal waste collection systems are overwhelmed or absent in most rural areas. Informal dumpsites operate on the edges of cities, nature reserves, and agricultural land with no environmental management, no containment, and no end in sight.

The result is a landscape increasingly contaminated by a material that does not biodegrade. Plastic does not disappear. It fragments. It persists. And it enters every living system it touches.

What Happens to Wildlife

Across the Maasai Mara, the Serengeti, and the vast savannah corridors of East Africa, plastic waste has infiltrated feeding grounds, water sources, and nesting sites. The consequences for wildlife are severe and well-documented.

Ingestion. Herbivores — including elephants, zebras, wildebeest, and giraffes — mistake plastic bags and packaging for vegetation, particularly when waste is mixed with organic matter at dump sites. Once ingested, plastic creates blockages in the digestive system that are almost always fatal. Studies in Kenya have found plastic in the stomach contents of wildlife across multiple species. In some cases, animals starve to death with full stomachs — full of material that provides zero nutrition and cannot be passed.

Entanglement. Birds, reptiles, and smaller mammals become entangled in plastic packaging, netting, and discarded fishing line. For species that nest on the ground or near water — including several critically endangered bird species found only in East Africa — entanglement is a leading cause of mortality.

Habitat degradation. Open dump sites on the edges of protected areas act as attractants, drawing wildlife into dangerous proximity with human settlements. The resulting human-wildlife conflict — like the elephant attack in Kenya — creates pressure for culling, relocation, and further habitat restriction. Plastic waste is not just killing individual animals. It is reshaping the relationship between wildlife and the landscapes they depend on.

The Livestock Crisis

Beyond protected areas, the impact on livestock is catastrophic — and directly affects the livelihoods of millions of African farmers and pastoralists.

Across Ethiopia, Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda, cattle, goats, and sheep regularly ingest plastic as they graze on land contaminated with waste. Studies across Ethiopian abattoirs have found plastic foreign bodies in 40–52% of cattle examined at slaughter. In some animals, the plastic content weighed several kilograms.

The economic impact is devastating. Animals that ingest significant amounts of plastic become visibly ill, lose condition, produce less milk, and frequently die before reaching market weight. For smallholder farmers operating on narrow margins, the loss of a single animal can be the difference between a viable season and debt.

This is not a theoretical risk. It is happening now, across millions of smallholdings, in communities that have no formal waste collection, no recycling infrastructure, and no alternative to watching their animals die from a problem they did not create.

Soil Contamination and Agriculture

The impact of plastic on land extends below the surface.

As plastic fragments under UV radiation and physical stress, it breaks into microplastics — particles smaller than 5mm that are effectively invisible to the naked eye. These microplastics accumulate in soil, altering its structure, reducing water retention, and disrupting the microbial communities that make fertile land possible.

Research has found that microplastic contamination in agricultural soil is now more pervasive than microplastic contamination in the ocean. For East Africa — where smallholder farming supports the majority of rural populations and where soil health is the foundation of food security — this is not an abstract environmental concern. It is a direct threat to the agricultural systems that feed the region.

Plastic burned in open dumps — the most common form of waste disposal across much of Africa — releases toxic compounds including dioxins and furans that deposit directly onto agricultural land and into groundwater. Communities living near dump sites experience elevated rates of respiratory illness, skin conditions, and cancers linked to chemical exposure from burning plastic.

The Intersection with Climate

Land-based plastic waste is also a climate issue — though it rarely gets discussed in those terms.

Open burning of plastic waste is one of the most carbon-intensive forms of waste disposal. It releases not only CO2 but short-lived climate pollutants including black carbon and methane, which have warming effects many times more potent than CO2 over short time horizons.

For a continent that contributes less than 4% of global greenhouse gas emissions but faces some of the most severe climate impacts — including the droughts, floods, and agricultural disruption already being felt across East Africa — the injustice is acute. Africa is not the source of the global plastic crisis. But it bears a disproportionate share of the consequences.
