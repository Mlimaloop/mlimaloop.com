The Tourism Economy's Plastic Problem — And Why East Africa's Hotels and Lodges Need MlimaLoop

East Africa's tourism economy is worth billions. It serves millions of visitors every year across some of the most iconic landscapes on the planet. And it generates enormous volumes of plastic waste with almost no certified local destination for recovery. Here is the opportunity nobody in the tourism industry is talking about loudly enough.

By MlimaLoop Team
MLIMALOOP

Every year, approximately one million people attempt to climb Kilimanjaro.

They arrive in Arusha, they kit out at the gear shops on Boma Road, they drive to the gates at Marangu or Machame or Lemosho, and they begin the ascent of Africa's highest peak. Over five to nine days, each climber consumes water. Lots of it. The standard advice is three to four litres per day at altitude. For a seven-day climb, that is twenty-one to twenty-eight litres per person. In plastic bottles.

Kilimanjaro plastic estimate: Approximately 1 million climbers per year Average water consumption: approximately 25 litres per climb At 10 grams per 500ml bottle: approximately 250 tonnes of PET plastic per year From a single mountain. Before safari vehicles, lodges, camps, and supply chains.

That is before the safari vehicles. Before the lodge restaurants. Before the bottled water served at every meal in every camp across the Serengeti, Ngorongoro, Tarangire, and Lake Manyara. Before the plastic packaging in the supply chains that serve hundreds of hotels, lodges, tented camps, and safari operators across northern Tanzania.

East Africa's tourism economy is one of the most valuable on the African continent. And it has a plastic waste problem that the industry is aware of, uncomfortable about, and almost entirely without local solutions for.

The Scale of the Tourism Economy

Tanzania's tourism sector generates approximately three billion dollars in revenue annually, making it one of the country's largest foreign exchange earners and one of the most significant economic sectors in the region.

The northern circuit alone, connecting Arusha to Kilimanjaro, the Serengeti, Ngorongoro Crater, Lake Manyara, and Tarangire, receives the majority of Tanzania's international visitors. In a strong year, the broader northern circuit and Kilimanjaro climbing economy together account for well over a million visitor arrivals.

These visitors come primarily from Europe, North America, and increasingly Asia. They are, by global standards, high-spending, environmentally conscious, and increasingly vocal about the sustainability credentials of the operators they choose. The traveller who selects a carbon-neutral lodge over a conventional one, or chooses a climbing operator committed to above-minimum porter wages over one that is not, is a real and growing market segment.

They also generate plastic waste at a rate significantly above the regional average, because their consumption patterns reflect the consumption patterns of the wealthy markets they come from, transplanted into a waste management environment that is, in most cases, essentially absent.

What Currently Happens to the Plastic

The honest answer is: not much that is certifiable.

The larger, more sophisticated lodges and camps in Tanzania have waste management programmes of varying quality. Some sort waste. Some compact plastic for collection. Some have relationships with informal aggregators who collect periodically. A small number have invested in community recycling programmes as part of their corporate social responsibility activities.

But none of them can currently demonstrate, to the standard required by international sustainability certification bodies, that the plastic waste generated on their property has been collected, processed, and converted into certified recycled material with a fully documented chain of custody.

This matters for two reasons.

First, the major international sustainability certifications that premium tourism operators seek — Travelife, Rainforest Alliance, Global Sustainable Tourism Council certification — increasingly require demonstrable waste management practices rather than aspirational policies. A lodge that sorts its plastic into labelled bins but cannot document where that plastic goes after collection is not meeting the certification standard. It is performing the appearance of waste management without the substance.

Second, the corporate travel and events market, which accounts for a significant proportion of high-end lodge revenue in East Africa, is increasingly subject to procurement sustainability requirements. A multinational corporation booking an incentive trip for five hundred executives is increasingly asking what the sustainability credentials of the accommodation are. A lodge that cannot demonstrate certified waste management is at a disadvantage in this market relative to one that can.

The Certification Gap

International tourism sustainability certification requires documented, verifiable waste management. Not a policy. Not an intention. Evidence.

Specifically, the most rigorous standards require weight-based collection records showing what volumes of each waste stream leave the property, documentation of the destination of each waste stream, and for recyclable materials, evidence that the material has reached a certified processor and been converted into certified output.

The last requirement is the one that currently cannot be met in northern Tanzania. A lodge can sort its plastic, weigh it, and document its collection. But without a certified processing facility that can provide GRS-verified output documentation and plastic credit certificates traceable to the lodge's waste, the chain of custody is incomplete.

MlimaLoop closes that gap. A lodge that contracts with MlimaLoop for plastic waste collection receives, for every tonne of PET collected from its property, GRS chain of custody documentation, plastic credit certificates tradeable on recognised registries, and the ability to make a verified, independently audited claim that its plastic waste has been converted into certified recycled material.

This is not just waste management. It is a sustainability asset. Something the lodge can include in its certification applications, its sustainability reports, its marketing materials, and its responses to corporate procurement questionnaires.

The Kilimanjaro Connection

The plastic waste generated on Kilimanjaro itself deserves specific attention.

The mountain is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. It is the most iconic trekking destination in Africa. It attracts visitors from across the world who come specifically because of its natural grandeur and ecological significance. And it generates significant plastic waste at every stage of the climbing experience.

At the trailheads, the gear shops, the briefing centres. On the trail itself, where water bottles accumulate at campsites. At the summit camps where altitude sickness, cold, and exhaustion mean that proper waste management is the last thing on anyone's mind. At the descent points where tired climbers dispose of whatever they are carrying in whatever way is most convenient.

MlimaLoop's co-founder Zidane Juma has guided on Kilimanjaro for over a decade through Vertical Sky. He knows the mountain's waste streams, the informal collection networks that operate around its base, and the operators who have the relationships and the motivation to formalise their waste management practices. This is not a cold commercial opportunity. It is a known landscape with known people and known problems that MlimaLoop is specifically positioned to address.

A certified collection network that operates from the Kilimanjaro gate areas to MlimaLoop's Arusha facility — documenting every kilogram, paying collectors fairly, issuing plastic credit certificates back to the lodges and operators that generated the waste — is a closed loop that serves the tourism economy, the collector community, and the mountain simultaneously.

The Lodge Partnership Model

MlimaLoop's approach to tourism sector partnerships is built around a simple commercial proposition.

A lodge or camp signs a waste collection agreement with MlimaLoop. MlimaLoop provides a buying station or scheduled collection service for the property's sorted PET waste. The lodge receives, for every tonne collected, GRS chain of custody certificates and plastic credit certificates that it can use for sustainability certification, reporting, and marketing.

The lodge does not need to invest in processing equipment. It does not need to establish its own recycling programme. It needs to sort its plastic, present it for collection, and receive the documentation that converts that action into a certifiable sustainability claim.

For lodges already investing in sustainability certification and sustainability marketing, which is most of the premium sector, this is a straightforward value exchange. The waste has to go somewhere. MlimaLoop is the certified somewhere it has been waiting for.

The Bigger Picture

The tourism economy of northern Tanzania is not just a source of plastic waste. It is a potential driver of the collection economics that make MlimaLoop's Arusha operation viable at an earlier stage than a purely residential collection model would allow.

Tourism operators generate concentrated, consistent, relatively clean PET waste streams. They are motivated buyers of the sustainability documentation that MlimaLoop produces. They have relationships with international certification bodies and corporate customers that value that documentation. And they have the financial capacity to pay for collection services that a subsistence collector community does not.

The partnership between MlimaLoop and the northern Tanzania tourism economy is not charity on either side. It is a commercial alignment between a waste generator that needs a certified destination and a processor that needs a reliable, high-quality feedstock stream.

The mountain generates the plastic. MlimaLoop closes the loop. The tourists who climb it go home knowing that the bottles they drank from on Kilimanjaro did not end up in the Pangani River.

That is a story worth telling. And a supply chain worth building.
